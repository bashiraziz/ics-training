Why the ICS Exists: FAR 52.216-7​

The incurred cost submission (ICS) is not a voluntary exercise. It is a contract requirement built into every cost-reimbursable and time-and-materials contract through the "Allowable Cost and Payment" clause — FAR 52.216-7.

When this clause appears in a contract, the contractor is obligated to:

  1. Bill only allowable, allocable, and reasonable costs during contract performance (using provisional rates).
  2. Submit a final indirect cost rate proposal — the ICS — within six months of the close of each fiscal year in which the contract was active.
  3. Repay the government for any overbillings, or receive payment for underbillings, once final rates are established.

FAR 52.216-7 is what closes the loop between provisional (estimated) billing rates used throughout the year and the actual costs incurred.

What "Allowable Cost" Means in the Payment Context​

FAR 52.216-7 ties payment to FAR Part 31 cost principles. A cost is allowable for reimbursement if it meets all five criteria at FAR 31.201-2:

CriterionWhat It Means
ReasonablenessA prudent person would incur this cost under similar circumstances
AllocabilityThe cost benefits the contract or can be fairly distributed to it
StandardsComplies with CAS or GAAP as applicable
Contract termsNot excluded by specific contract provisions
FAR Part 31Not expressly unallowable under a FAR 31.205-X cost principle

All five criteria must be satisfied. Costs that fail any one are unallowable and must be excluded from the ICS pools before computing claimed rates.

The Rate Framework: FAR 42.705 and 42.703​

Two additional FAR sections govern how indirect rates are established:

FAR 42.703 — Basic Agreement on Indirect Cost Rates: This provision allows contracting officers and contractors to negotiate a basic agreement establishing the indirect rate structure — which pools exist, how they are defined, and what bases they use. This is important because the ICS must be consistent with the agreed rate structure. If a contractor changes pool definitions year-over-year, it must explain the change.

FAR 42.705 — Final Indirect Cost Rates: This is the provision that governs how final rates are determined after the ICS is submitted. It gives the cognizant federal agency (usually DCAA for audit, then the ACO for rate negotiation) the authority to establish final rates either by agreement with the contractor or unilaterally. Once final rates are set, they apply to all contracts subject to FAR 52.216-7 for that fiscal year.

The ACO vs. the PCO​

Two contracting officers touch an ICS, and understanding their distinct roles prevents confusion:

Procuring Contracting Officer (PCO): Awards and administers the individual contract. The PCO ensures FAR 52.216-7 is in the contract but is generally not involved in the ICS audit process after award.

Administrative Contracting Officer (ACO): Takes over contract administration after award. The ACO is the contractor's primary point of contact for ICS-related matters. The ACO receives the ICS submission, coordinates with DCAA, and ultimately negotiates and executes the final rate agreement. For most contractors, the ACO is stationed at DCMA (Defense Contract Management Agency).

When DCAA issues a deficiency letter or requests clarifications, copies go to the ACO. When final rates are agreed, the ACO issues a rate agreement that applies across all contracts for that year.

The six-month filing deadline (June 30 for calendar-year contractors) is firm. Missing it can trigger a unilateral rate determination by the ACO, which is rarely favorable to the contractor.

Provisional Rates vs. Final Rates​

Throughout the year, contractors bill using provisional billing rates — estimates established at the start of the year (or negotiated with the ACO) that approximate what the final indirect rates will be. These are placeholders. They allow the contractor to get paid during performance without waiting for year-end actuals.

The ICS computes the final actual rates from real data. The difference — final rate minus provisional rate, applied to the actual allocation base — is the variance that must be settled. This settlement is the economic heart of the ICS process.
