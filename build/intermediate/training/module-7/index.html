Intermediate Module 7 - Common ICS Deficiencies

Module progress

What a Deficiency Letter Means​

When DCAA determines that a submitted ICS is inadequate, it issues a formal deficiency letter to the contractor (with a copy to the ACO). The letter identifies each deficiency and specifies that the six-month filing clock has stopped as of the date the submission was received — and will not restart until an adequate resubmission is made.

From a practical standpoint, a deficiency letter means:

  • The audit cannot begin until the deficiencies are corrected.
  • Contractor resources must be diverted to fix and resubmit.
  • The timeline to final rates — and billing adjustments — is extended.
  • If the original submission was late, the deficiency period does not excuse the lateness.

Understanding the eight most common deficiencies is the most efficient way to prevent them.

Deficiency 1: Missing Contractor Certification (Schedule H Not Signed)​

What DCAA looks for: A signed Schedule H with the certification language required by FAR 42.703-2, signed by an official with authority to bind the company.

How it manifests: The certification page is missing entirely, it is signed by someone without apparent authority (e.g., a staff accountant rather than a CFO or President), or it covers the wrong fiscal year.

Prevention: Make Schedule H the last step in your preparation process — after all numbers are final — but build it into your checklist so it cannot be overlooked. Confirm the signer's authority in advance.

Deficiency 2: Schedule Totals Don't Reconcile​

What DCAA looks for: Schedule A pool totals = Schedule B pool totals. Schedule C direct costs = the allocation bases used in Schedule A. Schedule E variances = Schedule D computed variances.

How it manifests: Off-by amounts between A and B (often because B was prepared from budget data and A from actuals, or because a late journal entry was captured in one but not the other). Mismatched allocation bases.

Prevention: Prepare a formal reconciliation worksheet — for each pool, show the G/L source → Schedule B total → Schedule A total in a three-column format. Any gap must have an explanation before submission.

Deficiency 3: Unallowable Costs Not Properly Segregated​

What DCAA looks for: Evidence that all expressly unallowable costs have been identified, removed from the claimed pools, and documented in the ICS schedules.

How it manifests: Entertainment, lobbying, or interest expense appears in a claimed pool account with no deduction on Schedule B. Or the deduction amount on Schedule B does not match the sub-ledger of identified unallowable costs.

Prevention: Maintain a running unallowable cost log throughout the year. At year-end, reconcile the log to the G/L and to Schedule B. Never submit an ICS without having a documented answer to: "What unallowable costs were in each pool, and how were they removed?"

Deficiency 4: Allocation Base Inconsistent with Disclosed Accounting Practices​

What DCAA looks for: The allocation base described in the ICS (or negotiated in a prior rate agreement) must match the base actually used to compute the claimed rates.

How it manifests: A contractor whose prior ICS used direct labor dollars to allocate overhead switches to direct labor hours without notification. Or a contractor uses a budgeted base instead of the actual year-end base.

Prevention: Document your allocation base methodology in writing (your accounting manual or rate submission narrative). If you change a base, notify the ACO before filing and provide a justification.

Deficiency 5: Missing Supporting Schedules or Sub-Schedules​

What DCAA looks for: A complete submission includes all schedules A through H, plus any contractor-specific sub-schedules needed to explain pool contents (e.g., a breakdown of pension costs, a detail of IR&D/B&P projects, a lease cost schedule).

How it manifests: Schedules C, F, or G are omitted. The contractor provides only a two-schedule packet (A and B) and calls it complete. Large cost elements in Schedule B (e.g., $500,000 in "other indirect costs") have no backup schedule.

Prevention: Use the DCAA-published ICS checklist (available on the DCAA website) as a minimum completeness guide. For any material cost element, ask: "Would an auditor be able to verify this number from what I've provided?"

Deficiency 6: Direct Costs Don't Reconcile to Contract Billing Records​

What DCAA looks for: The direct costs in Schedule C should match — or be explainable relative to — what was actually billed on each contract during the year.

How it manifests: A contract shows $500,000 in direct labor in Schedule C but only $420,000 was billed. Without a Schedule E entry explaining the underbilling, the gap looks like a records inconsistency.

Prevention: Prepare Schedule E before finalizing Schedule C. The act of computing the over/underbilling by contract will surface any mismatches between accounting records and billing records.

Deficiency 7: Indirect Cost Pools Inconsistent with Prior Years Without Explanation​

What DCAA looks for: Year-over-year consistency in how pools are defined and populated. If the structure changes, DCAA expects a written explanation.

How it manifests: The fringe pool suddenly includes costs that were in overhead last year. A new pool appears without explanation. Cost elements move between G&A and overhead with no narrative.

Prevention: Include a brief "Changes from Prior Year" narrative section in your ICS. Even a one-paragraph explanation of any structural changes will typically satisfy DCAA's consistency inquiry.

Deficiency 8: Missing Organizational Chart​

What DCAA looks for: An org chart (Schedule G) that reflects the organizational structure during the fiscal year covered by the ICS.

How it manifests: The org chart is omitted, or the org chart provided is the current structure, which differs from the structure during the year in question (e.g., after a reorganization).

Prevention: Save a dated copy of the org chart at fiscal year-end. Include it as Schedule G without modification.

Pre-Submission Checklist​

Before submitting an ICS, confirm each item below:

  • Schedule H is signed by an authorized official covering the correct fiscal year
  • Schedule A pool totals tie exactly to Schedule B pool totals (for each pool)
  • Schedule A allocation bases tie to Schedule C direct cost totals
  • Schedule D rate variances times bases equal Schedule E contract-level variances
  • All FAR 31.205 unallowable costs are identified and deducted on Schedule B
  • An unallowable cost sub-ledger exists and reconciles to Schedule B deductions
  • All supporting schedules (C, D, E, F, G, H) are present and complete
  • Allocation base methodology is consistent with prior year or change is documented
  • Org chart reflects structure during the fiscal year (not current structure if changed)
  • Any material Schedule B line items have supporting backup available
