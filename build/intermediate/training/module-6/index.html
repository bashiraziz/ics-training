Intermediate Module 6 - Unallowable Cost Identification in ICS

Module progress
Module id: module6

The Governing Rule: FAR 31.201-6​

FAR 31.201-6 is the enforcement provision for unallowable costs. It states that unallowable costs must be identified and excluded from any billing, claim, or proposal submitted to the government. This applies directly to the ICS: every dollar of unallowable cost that flows through a contractor's books must be removed from the indirect cost pools before the claimed rates are computed.

There are three specific requirements under FAR 31.201-6:

  1. Identification: Unallowable costs must be formally identified in the accounting records — not just known informally.
  2. Exclusion: They must be excluded from any claimed amount. They cannot appear in Schedule A or B as "claimed" costs.
  3. Tracking: They must be maintained in a way that allows DCAA to verify the exclusion (separate accounts, sub-ledger codes, or a reconciliation schedule).

Failing to exclude a known unallowable cost from an ICS is not a minor error. It is a compliance failure that can result in cost disallowance, interest charges, and — if the cost is "expressly unallowable" — significant penalties.

Expressly Unallowable vs. Disputed Unallowable​

The distinction between "expressly unallowable" and "disputed unallowable" has major penalty implications.

Expressly Unallowable: A cost that is specifically named and declared unallowable by statute, regulation, or contract terms — with no ambiguity. Entertainment (FAR 31.205-14) and lobbying (FAR 31.205-22) are classic examples. The FAR says they are unallowable, period.

Disputed Unallowable: A cost that a contractor believes is allowable but DCAA disagrees with — or where there is genuine ambiguity in how the cost principle applies to a specific situation. A travel cost that slightly exceeds per diem might be disputed. An executive bonus that arguably meets the reasonableness test might be disputed.

Why it matters — the penalty rule: Under FAR 42.709, if a contractor includes an expressly unallowable cost in an ICS, the government may assess a penalty equal to two times the amount of the unallowable cost (plus interest). This penalty is on top of the disallowance. For disputed costs, the penalty does not apply (though the cost is still disallowed if DCAA prevails).

This penalty exposure is why contractors who have entertainment or lobbying expenses in their G/L must be especially careful to segregate and exclude these amounts — even if they are small.

How to Properly Segregate Unallowable Costs​

DCAA looks for a systematic, documented approach to unallowable cost identification — not ad hoc adjustments at year-end. Best practices:

Separate accounts in the general ledger: The cleanest approach is to use dedicated G/L account codes for unallowable costs (e.g., Account 6210 — Entertainment — Unallowable, Account 6215 — Entertainment — Allowable). This way, the costs are never co-mingled in the claimed pool to begin with.

Sub-ledger or memorandum account: If the G/L doesn't segregate by allowability, maintain a parallel sub-ledger that flags each unallowable transaction. This sub-ledger must tie to Schedule B.

The DCAA test: During an audit, DCAA will:

  1. Obtain the contractor's trial balance and list of accounts coded as unallowable.
  2. Trace those amounts to the "Less Unallowable" columns in Schedule B.
  3. Pull a sample of costs from the claimed (allowable) accounts and test whether any unallowable costs were inadvertently included.
  4. Check whether any accounts contain a mix of allowable and unallowable items without proper allocation.

Step 3 is where contractors get into trouble. Even if the known entertainment costs are excluded, if the auditor pulls a sample from the "Travel" account and finds dinner receipts that look like entertainment, those costs may be disallowed and — if treated as expressly unallowable — may trigger penalties.

Practical Guidance​

  • Review the FAR 31.205 cost principles at least annually and compare them to your chart of accounts.
  • Train accounting staff to flag and code unallowable costs at the point of entry, not at year-end.
  • Document the rationale for borderline costs (e.g., a business meal that is clearly work-related, not entertainment).
  • When in doubt about the allowability of a cost, segregate it and note it as "potentially unallowable" pending review — do not include it in the claimed pool.
