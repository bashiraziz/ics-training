Beginner Module 6 - Reconciling Claimed vs. Billed Costs

Module progress
Module id: module6

The Purpose of Reconciliation​

After the fiscal year ends and final indirect rates are established, the contractor must answer a straightforward question for every government contract: Did we bill more or less than we actually incurred?

That question is answered by Schedule E — the reconciliation of claimed versus billed costs. This is the financial settlement document of the ICS. Everything else in the package supports getting Schedule E right.

What Schedule E Shows​

For each cost-reimbursable or T&M contract active during the fiscal year, Schedule E presents three columns:

  1. Total costs billed — what the contractor invoiced using provisional indirect rates during the year
  2. Total costs claimed — what the contractor actually incurred, expressed at final indirect rates
  3. Variance — the difference (billed minus claimed)

A positive variance (billed > claimed) is an overbilling — the contractor received more money than it earned. The contractor owes the government a refund.

A negative variance (billed < claimed) is an underbilling — the contractor invoiced less than it actually incurred. The contractor can submit a final invoice for the remainder.

A Numerical Example​

Suppose a contractor has three active government contracts at year-end. Here is how Schedule E might look:

ContractCosts Billed (Provisional)Costs Claimed (Final)VariancePosition
Contract A (CPFF)$420,000$395,000+$25,000Overbilled
Contract B (T&M)$310,000$327,500-$17,500Underbilled
Contract C (CPFF)$175,000$175,000$0Balanced
Total$905,000$897,500+$7,500Net overbilling

In this example, Contract A was overbilled by $25,000 (the provisional overhead rate was higher than the final rate). Contract B was underbilled by $17,500 (perhaps the G&A rate came in higher than the provisional estimate). Contract C happened to be exactly right.

Across all three contracts, the contractor has a net overbilling of $7,500. After final rates are established, the contractor would typically credit the government $7,500 — either through a refund check or by reducing a future invoice.

Why Individual Contracts Matter​

The reconciliation is done by contract, not just in the aggregate. This matters because:

  • Different contracts may have different funding limits, and overbillings on one cannot automatically offset underbillings on another without contracting officer authorization
  • Some contracts may be with different agencies — overbillings on a DoD contract can't automatically be applied to NASA underbillings
  • Final billing adjustments must be processed through each contract's payment mechanism

Contractors should not simply net overbillings and underbillings across all contracts and send one check. Each contract's CO must be involved in settling that contract's variance.

What Drives Variances​

Rate variances — the root cause of Schedule E variances — typically come from:

  • Actual overhead costs running higher or lower than the provisional estimate
  • The allocation base (direct labor) growing or shrinking relative to the forecast — if direct labor was lower than expected, overhead spread across a smaller base means a higher rate
  • New indirect costs added during the year that weren't in the provisional estimate
  • Unplanned changes in employee headcount affecting fringe benefit pool costs

Understanding the source of the variance helps the contractor explain it to DCAA and the CO during the audit phase.

The True-Up Billing Process​

Once final rates are approved (either through audit agreement or DCAA acceptance), the true-up follows these steps:

  1. Final rates are documented in a rate agreement or DCAA audit report
  2. Schedule E variances are confirmed using the approved final rates
  3. For overbilled contracts: The contracting officer issues a demand for repayment, or the contractor applies a credit to an open invoice
  4. For underbilled contracts: The contractor submits a final indirect cost billing using a standard invoice referencing the approved final rates
  5. Closeout: Once settled, the contract can proceed to financial closeout

There is typically a time limit on collecting underbillings. Contractors should not wait years to pursue final billing — delays can create complications with contract funding, closeout, and CO availability.

Common Errors in Schedule E​

  • Using wrong provisional rates — using estimated rates rather than the actual rates in effect during the billing period
  • Missing contracts — failing to include all active contracts, including those that ended mid-year
  • Arithmetic errors — transposition mistakes that cause the schedule not to reconcile to Schedule C
  • Ignoring funding ceilings — claiming final costs that exceed the contract's funded ceiling, which requires a separate modification before the additional costs can be claimed

Getting Schedule E right is essential. Errors here are what contractors actually pay for — or get paid for.
