Beginner Module 3 - Provisional vs. Final Rates

Module progress
Module id: module3

Why Two Sets of Rates?​

Government contracting doesn't wait until the end of the year to pay contractors. Invoices go out monthly or even more frequently. But indirect costs — overhead, fringe benefits, G&A — can't be calculated precisely until the fiscal year is complete and all the books are closed. That creates a practical problem: how do you bill indirect costs when you don't yet know what they'll be?

The answer is a two-step system: provisional rates for billing during the year, and final rates established after year-end. The ICS is the mechanism that produces the final rates and settles the difference.

Provisional Billing Rates​

A provisional billing rate is a pre-approved estimated indirect rate used to calculate and invoice indirect costs on government contracts throughout the fiscal year. Provisional rates are set in one of two ways:

  • Negotiated with DCAA or the Contracting Officer before the fiscal year begins, based on the contractor's forward-pricing estimate
  • Carried forward from prior-year actuals when no formal negotiation takes place

Provisional rates appear on invoices as a percentage applied to a base (usually direct labor). They are temporary by definition — they are placeholders until actual costs are known.

Example: If your provisional fringe benefit rate is 32%, every invoice includes 32 cents of fringe for every $1.00 of direct labor billed. You collect that money throughout the year even though you won't know your actual fringe costs until the year ends.

Final Indirect Rates​

Final rates are computed after fiscal year-end using actual costs incurred and actual allocation bases from the contractor's accounting records. The ICS is the document that presents this calculation.

Final rates go through one of three outcomes:

  1. Audit and negotiation — DCAA reviews the ICS and negotiates final rates with the contractor and contracting officer
  2. Audit and unilateral determination — If the parties can't agree, the contracting officer may set rates unilaterally
  3. Rate agreement — Sometimes rates are accepted as submitted or agreed without a full field audit

Once final rates are established, they are permanently fixed for that fiscal year. They cannot be revised later (absent fraud or clerical error).

Understanding Rate Variances​

A rate variance occurs when the final rate differs from the provisional rate. This is expected — it would be remarkable if estimates perfectly matched actuals. Variances are either:

  • Favorable to the government (overbilling): The provisional rate was higher than the final rate. The contractor billed more indirect cost than was actually incurred and must refund the difference.
  • Favorable to the contractor (underbilling): The provisional rate was lower than the final rate. The contractor billed less than it actually incurred and can invoice the additional amount.

Numerical Example: Overhead Rate True-Up​

Suppose a contractor sets a provisional overhead rate of 85% applied to direct labor. During the year, they invoice $1,000,000 in direct labor and collect 85% × $1,000,000 = $850,000 in overhead.

At year-end, the ICS reveals:

ItemAmount
Actual overhead pool costs$780,000
Actual direct labor base$1,000,000
Final overhead rate78%
Overhead billed at provisional rate (85%)$850,000
Overhead earned at final rate (78%)$780,000
Overbilling — contractor must refund$70,000

The contractor collected $70,000 more than the actual overhead warranted. The ICS documents this, and the contracting officer issues a demand for repayment — or the contractor applies the credit against future invoices.

The True-Up Process​

"True-up" refers to the billing adjustment that occurs once final rates are established. The steps are:

  1. ICS is submitted and final rates are determined (through audit or agreement)
  2. A contract-by-contract reconciliation is prepared (Schedule E)
  3. For each contract, the difference between billed costs and claimed costs is calculated
  4. Net overbillings across all contracts: contractor issues a credit or refund
  5. Net underbillings: contractor submits a final invoice for the additional amount

True-up settlements are typically processed through the contracting officer, not directly with DCAA. DCAA establishes the rates; the CO handles the money.

Why Rates Matter Beyond the True-Up​

Final rates from a completed ICS also feed into:

  • Forward-pricing for future contract bids (prior-year actuals inform next year's provisional rates)
  • Incurred cost audits that examine whether claimed costs comply with FAR Part 31
  • Overhead rate trends that DCAA tracks year-over-year and may use as audit triggers

Getting final rates right is not just about one year's settlement — it shapes how DCAA views your company going forward.
