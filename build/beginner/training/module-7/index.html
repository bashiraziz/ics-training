Beginner Module 7 - DCAA and the ICS

Module progress

Who Is DCAA?​

The Defense Contract Audit Agency (DCAA) is an independent agency within the Department of Defense responsible for performing contract audits on behalf of DoD and, by agreement, other federal agencies. DCAA has no contract authority — it cannot approve, reject, or modify contracts. It audits costs and provides findings and recommendations to contracting officers, who make the final decisions.

DCAA's jurisdiction covers contractors with cost-reimbursable and T&M contracts with DoD and other agencies that request DCAA audit services. For the ICS, DCAA is the primary reviewer and auditor.

Step 1: Submission and the Adequacy Review​

When a contractor submits an ICS, DCAA's first action is a completeness and adequacy check — sometimes called a desk review. This is not a full audit. It is a structured checklist review to determine whether the submission contains all required elements.

DCAA's adequacy checklist evaluates:

  • Are all required schedules present?
  • Is Schedule H (the certification) signed by an authorized official?
  • Can indirect rates be computed from the data presented?
  • Do direct costs on Schedule C reconcile to total claimed costs?
  • Are indirect pools and bases consistently defined?

If the submission is adequate, DCAA acknowledges receipt and the submission enters the audit queue. If it is inadequate, DCAA returns it with a written deficiency notice. The submission clock effectively restarts — the contractor must refile, and the original submission date no longer counts.

Step 2: The Audit (or Acceptance)​

After adequacy is confirmed, DCAA has three main paths:

  1. No further audit action — For low-risk submissions (small dollar amounts, strong track record, no risk indicators), DCAA may accept the submission with no further work and recommend the CO approve proposed rates. This is more common for very small contractors.

  2. Limited-scope audit — DCAA focuses on specific areas of risk rather than testing everything. Common limited-scope areas include G&A pool composition, fringe benefit calculations, and unallowable cost exclusions.

  3. Full incurred cost audit — A comprehensive examination of all schedules, cost elements, and supporting documentation. Full audits are typical for contractors with large dollar volumes, rate fluctuations, prior audit findings, or complex cost structures.

What DCAA Looks For​

Regardless of audit scope, DCAA auditors consistently test for the following:

Mathematical accuracy. Can the rates on Schedule A be reproduced from the data on Schedule B? Do Schedule C totals add up? Does Schedule E reconcile to Schedule D?

Proper cost classification. Are indirect costs genuinely indirect (not direct charges to a specific contract)? Are direct costs genuinely direct (not expenses that benefit all contracts)?

Exclusion of unallowable costs. Have all FAR 31.205 unallowable costs been removed from claimed pools? DCAA will sample expenses in each indirect pool and trace them to source documents to verify allowability.

Supporting documentation. Every cost claimed must be traceable to source documents — invoices, payroll records, lease agreements, general ledger entries. Costs without documentation cannot be allowed.

Consistency with prior years. DCAA compares current-year rates and cost structures to prior-year submissions. Unexplained significant changes are audit triggers.

Common Audit Triggers​

The following increase the likelihood and intensity of DCAA scrutiny:

  • Large rate variances year-over-year (e.g., overhead jumped from 72% to 105%)
  • Missing or incomplete Schedule H certification
  • Large claimed amounts in accounts with sparse documentation
  • New indirect cost pools or changes in allocation bases without explanation
  • History of prior audit findings, questioned costs, or late submissions
  • High G&A rate relative to industry norms
  • Subcontract costs without proper supporting agreements

The Audit Resolution Process​

If DCAA identifies questioned or disallowed costs, the process follows these steps:

  1. DCAA issues a draft audit report — The contractor receives findings and has an opportunity to respond with clarification or additional documentation.
  2. Contractor responds — The contractor can accept findings, provide documentation to resolve questions, or formally disagree with DCAA's position.
  3. DCAA issues a final audit report — Reflects any revisions based on the contractor's response. Recommended rate adjustments are included.
  4. Contracting officer makes the determination — The CO reviews DCAA's report and the contractor's position. The CO has authority to accept, modify, or reject DCAA's recommendations.
  5. Final rates are established — Through a negotiation or a unilateral CO determination.
  6. Settlement is processed — Based on final rates, Schedule E variances are resolved and billing adjustments are made.

Practical Tips for Working with DCAA​

  • Respond to DCAA requests promptly and professionally. Delays slow down rate settlement and can extend the withholding period.
  • Organize your supporting documentation before the audit begins — don't wait for requests to start gathering records.
  • If DCAA questions a cost and you believe it is allowable, respond in writing with the specific FAR citation supporting your position.
  • Understand that DCAA auditors are doing a job, not adversaries. Many findings are resolved through documentation rather than cost disallowance.
