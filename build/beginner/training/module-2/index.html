Beginner Module 2 - Who Must File and When

Module progress
Module id: module2

The Filing Threshold​

Not every government contractor must file an ICS. The obligation is triggered when a contractor has cost-reimbursable or time-and-materials contracts where the total costs expected to be incurred under those contracts exceed $250,000 in a fiscal year.

This threshold is applied contract-by-contract: if your only cost-type contract is a $180,000 cost-plus-fixed-fee (CPFF) contract, you are below the threshold and not required to file. If that same contract has $280,000 in expected costs, you must file.

The threshold was established in DCAA's guidance and is referenced in the FAR 52.216-7 clause requirements. When in doubt, consult your contracting officer — some agencies apply stricter thresholds or impose filing requirements contractually regardless of the dollar amount.

Which Contract Types Trigger Filing​

The ICS requirement applies to contracts where the government reimburses actual costs. Fixed-price contracts — where the price is set regardless of what you spend — do not trigger an ICS requirement because there are no provisional indirect rates to reconcile.

For IDIQ contracts, look at the individual task orders. A task order written as CPFF triggers filing; one written as FFP does not.

The Filing Deadline​

The ICS is due six months after the end of the contractor's fiscal year. For most contractors on a calendar fiscal year (January 1 – December 31), the deadline is June 30.

If your fiscal year ends on a different date, count six months from that date:

  • Fiscal year ending March 31 → ICS due September 30
  • Fiscal year ending June 30 → ICS due December 31
  • Fiscal year ending September 30 → ICS due March 31

The deadline applies to each fiscal year separately. You are not filing one cumulative document — each fiscal year gets its own ICS package.

Consequences of Late Filing​

Missing the deadline is not a paperwork technicality. DCAA has authority under the contract clause to recommend that the contracting officer withhold 5% of amounts invoiced on cost-type contracts until the ICS is submitted. This withhold can accumulate significantly on active contracts.

Additional consequences of late filing:

  • DCAA may classify the submission as inadequate and restart the clock
  • Interest on underbillings may be forfeited if the delay is contractor-caused
  • Persistent late filing can damage past performance ratings and affect future contract awards
  • The statute of limitations on settling rates may be affected

What Counts as "Filing"​

Simply sending paperwork is not enough. DCAA evaluates the submission for adequacy — confirming that all required schedules are present, the certification is signed, and the data is internally consistent. An inadequate submission is returned and treated as not filed for purposes of the deadline.

DCAA's adequacy checklist examines whether:

  • All required schedules are included and complete
  • The contractor's certificate (Schedule H) is signed by an authorized official
  • Direct and indirect costs reconcile to the general ledger
  • Indirect rates can be computed from the submitted data

Filing an adequate, complete ICS on time is the contractor's responsibility. Auditors will not complete the submission for you.

Quick Reference: Filing Checklist​

  • Identify all cost-reimbursable and T&M contracts active during the fiscal year
  • Confirm total expected costs exceed $250,000
  • Determine your fiscal year-end date
  • Calculate the deadline (fiscal year-end + 6 months)
  • Prepare all required schedules
  • Obtain an authorized signature on the contractor certificate
  • Submit to the cognizant DCAA field audit office
