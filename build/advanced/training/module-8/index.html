Advanced Module 8 - Advanced Case Study: Apex Federal Systems

Module progress
Module id: module8

Company Background​

Apex Federal Systems is a 400-person government contractor with $85 million in contract revenue for fiscal year under audit. The company operates two divisions and a home office:

  • Division 1 — IT Services: 220 employees, $52M in contract revenue, primarily cost-plus-fixed-fee and T&M contracts with DHS and DoD.
  • Division 2 — Engineering Services: 180 employees, $33M in contract revenue, mix of cost-plus and fixed-price with DoD research agencies.
  • Home Office: Executive leadership, Finance, HR, Legal, and Corporate BD. $6.2M in annual costs.

Apex is fully CAS-covered (CAS-covered contract awards exceeded $50M in the prior year). It has an active Disclosure Statement on file for each division. Division 1 uses a Total Cost Input G&A base; Division 2 uses a Value Added G&A base — a difference explicitly disclosed in each division's DS.

ICS Filing Structure​

Apex files three separate ICS submissions:

  1. Home Office ICS — $6.2M in home office costs allocated to the two divisions using a three-factor formula (payroll, revenue, assets).
  2. Division 1 ICS — IT Services indirect cost structure, including Division 1 overhead pools and its share of home office allocation.
  3. Division 2 ICS — Engineering Services indirect cost structure, similarly including its home office allocation share.

DCAA Audit: Year 1 Findings​

DCAA's adequacy review was completed in 45 days — both division ICS submissions were adequate on first submission. The desk review identified three risk areas, which field testing confirmed:

Finding 1: Entertainment Costs — $82,000 Questioned​

During desk review, DCAA identified $82,000 in a "Business Development Meals and Entertainment" account within Division 1's G&A pool. Field testing of 35 sampled transactions (totaling $61,000) found:

  • $48,000 consisted of restaurant meals and event tickets for government personnel — expressly unallowable entertainment under FAR 31.205-14.
  • $13,000 consisted of internal employee recognition events — potentially allowable morale costs under FAR 31.205-13, but documentation for three events ($8,200) did not identify the business purpose.
  • $21,000 (not tested) was scaled to the sample rate.

DCAA questioned the full $82,000.

Finding 2: Executive Compensation — $193,000 Questioned​

Apex's CEO compensation for Year 1 was $875,000 (base salary plus bonus). DCAA used the Radford Executive Survey for companies with $75M-$100M in revenue to establish a benchmark. The Radford benchmark for CEO total compensation at that size: $682,000.

DCAA questioned $193,000 as the excess above the benchmark ($875,000 - $682,000 = $193,000), citing FAR 31.205-6(p).

Finding 3: CAS Noncompliance — Allocation Base Change — $65,000 Impact​

Division 2's Disclosure Statement described its G&A allocation base as Value Added (total cost input minus subcontract costs and materials). During audit, DCAA discovered that Division 2 had changed its G&A base to a modified Value Added that also excluded other direct costs (ODCs) — a change not reflected in an amended Disclosure Statement.

The CAS noncompliance impact: under the disclosed Value Added method, G&A would have been allocated at 18.4%. Under the actual modified base, G&A was allocated at 20.1%. The difference overstated Division 2's G&A charges on cost-type contracts by approximately $65,000.

Total questioned costs in draft report: $340,000.

Apex's Rebuttal Strategy​

Apex submitted a management response 28 days after receiving the draft audit report.

Response to Finding 1 (Entertainment — $82,000):​

Apex conceded $48,000 for the government personnel meals and events — these were clearly unallowable and no defense was available. For the $13,000 in employee events, Apex provided after-the-fact documentation for $9,400 in events (minutes from company all-hands meetings, attendance records, and descriptions of business purpose). For the remaining $3,600 in events with no documentation, Apex accepted the disallowance. For the $21,000 scaled amount, Apex argued that the scaling was not appropriate — the untested transactions were from a different program's budget with distinct documentation practices. It offered to provide all supporting documentation for the untested items rather than accept a scaled disallowance.

Apex accepted: $51,600. Contested: $30,400.

Response to Finding 2 (Executive Compensation — $193,000):​

Apex argued that Radford was not the appropriate benchmark. It submitted a Willis Towers Watson survey using a technology-services industry peer group (rather than Radford's general industry peer group) and a McLagan survey of government IT contractors. Both surveys showed a 75th-percentile benchmark of $820,000 for a CEO at a $85M government IT company. Apex also documented the CEO's unique qualifications: 22 years in government IT, active security clearance at TS/SCI level, and personal relationships driving $30M in new contracts.

Apex contested: $193,000. Proposed: $55,000 disallowance (excess above $820,000 benchmark).

Response to Finding 3 (CAS Noncompliance — $65,000):​

Apex accepted the finding. The change in allocation base had occurred 18 months ago when Division 2 implemented a new ERP system. The DS amendment should have been filed at that time and was not. Apex agreed to file a DS amendment immediately, pay the $65,000 price adjustment on affected contracts, and implement a DS amendment checklist as part of its ICS preparation process.

Apex accepted: $65,000.

ACO Negotiation and Resolution​

The ACO received the final DCAA audit report, which maintained all $340,000 in questioned costs. The ACO convened a negotiation conference with Apex.

Finding 1 resolution: DCAA agreed to withdraw the $21,000 scaled amount after Apex provided documentation showing those items were allowable business promotion costs. The ACO accepted: $61,000 disallowed ($48,000 clear entertainment + $9,800 undocumented events + $3,200 adjusted rounding).

Finding 2 resolution: The ACO reviewed both compensation surveys submitted by Apex and found the WTW government IT contractor peer group more persuasive than the Radford general industry benchmark. The ACO established a benchmark of $795,000 and disallowed $80,000 ($875,000 - $795,000).

Finding 3 resolution: Apex's acceptance and immediate corrective action were noted. The $65,000 disallowance was confirmed. The DS amendment was filed and approved within 60 days of the negotiation conference.

Final Rate Agreement and Contract Billing Impact​

The ACO issued final indirect cost rate agreements for both divisions. With $206,000 in disallowed costs removed from the pools, the final rates were marginally lower than the provisional billing rates. DCAA calculated that the rate adjustments resulted in $89,000 in net overbillings across Division 1 cost-type contracts (the entertainment and compensation costs were in Division 1's G&A pool).

Apex repaid the $89,000 overbilling plus CDA interest of $4,200, totaling $93,200. The Division 2 CAS adjustment of $65,000 was handled separately under the CAS price adjustment clause in each affected contract.

With final rates established, 14 contracts that had been awaiting final rates were closed out within 90 days.

Lessons from the Apex Case Study​

  1. Accept the clear losses early. Conceding the $48,000 entertainment disallowance immediately built credibility with DCAA and the ACO on the more defensible items.
  2. Challenge the benchmark, not the principle. Attacking DCAA's use of the wrong compensation survey was far more effective than arguing that executive compensation limits were inappropriate.
  3. Fix CAS problems immediately. Accepting the noncompliance finding and filing the DS amendment promptly prevented the issue from expanding into subsequent audit years.
  4. Documentation matters at every level. The $21,000 scaled disallowance was recovered entirely through providing documentation — money that would have been lost without organized records.
  5. Multi-segment coordination is essential. Home office allocations, inter-segment consistency, and separate DS filings for each segment created multiple compliance checkpoints — each of which required attention.
