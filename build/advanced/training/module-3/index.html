Advanced Module 3 - Quick-Close vs. Full ICS

Module progress
Module id: module3

Two Paths to Final Rates​

Every ICS year must eventually be closed — meaning final indirect cost rates are established and all contract billings are reconciled to those final rates. There are two ways to get there:

  1. Full ICS audit — DCAA audits the submission, issues a report, and the ACO negotiates final rates with the contractor.
  2. Quick-close — The contractor and ACO agree to establish final rates without a full DCAA audit, relying on billing rates already agreed upon and a judgment that a full audit is not cost-effective.

The quick-close option exists because a thorough DCAA audit of a small contractor's ICS year may cost more in government time than the audit could possibly recover. FAR 42.708 provides the authority for this alternative.

Quick-Close Qualifying Conditions​

FAR 42.708 lists the conditions that must be satisfied before a quick-close is appropriate:

  1. No unsettled prior ICS years. The contractor must not have open, unresolved ICS years from prior periods. Quick-close is not available if you have a backlog of unsettled years that raises systemic risk.
  2. Low dollar threshold. The total unsettled direct costs for the ICS year must be below the threshold established by the head of the contracting activity. In practice, this often means a relatively small contract portfolio — typically contractors with limited cost-type contract volume are the best candidates.
  3. No known significant deficiencies. DCAA and the ACO must have no knowledge of material cost accounting deficiencies, estimating system weaknesses, or pending fraud referrals for that contractor.
  4. ACO and DCAA agreement. Both the ACO and DCAA must agree that a full audit is not warranted given the risk profile.

If any condition is missing, quick-close is off the table and the full audit path applies.

The Quick-Close Negotiation Process​

When all conditions are met, the quick-close process works as follows:

Step 1: Contractor requests quick-close. After filing the ICS, the contractor submits a written request to the ACO identifying that it believes the year qualifies for quick-close treatment.

Step 2: ACO solicits DCAA input. The ACO asks DCAA whether it has any objections or plans to audit the submission. DCAA reviews the contractor's profile — past audit findings, current billing rates, known risk factors.

Step 3: Rates agreed. If DCAA concurs, the ACO and contractor negotiate final rates. These are typically very close to the provisional billing rates already in use, adjusted only for any obvious variances identified during a desk review of the ICS.

Step 4: Rate agreement issued. The ACO issues a final indirect cost rate agreement (similar to an FPRA in structure). This closes the year.

Step 5: Contract closeout. With final rates established, individual contracts can be closed out, final vouchers submitted, and any overbilling/underbilling resolved.

What Documentation Is Still Required​

Quick-close does not mean no documentation. The contractor still must:

  • Submit a complete ICS in the standard format (Schedules A through O as applicable).
  • Provide the reconciliation of the ICS to financial statements.
  • Have records available if the ACO requests support for any rate element.

The key difference is that DCAA does not conduct field testing of invoices, timesheets, or subcontract files. The ACO relies on the overall adequacy of the submission and the billing rate history.

What the Contractor Gives Up​

By accepting quick-close, the contractor waives the right to later claim additional costs that were not captured in the agreed final rates. If you realize six months after quick-close that you under-billed $200,000 in allowable costs, you have no recourse — the rates are final and binding.

This is the primary risk of quick-close. Contractors with volatile cost structures, large unresolved allowability questions, or pending claims should think carefully before agreeing to quick-close.

The contrast is stark. For a contractor with modest cost-type contract exposure, waiting 5 years for a DCAA audit result is an enormous administrative burden. Quick-close, when available, dramatically reduces that burden.

When Full Audit Is the Better Path​

Quick-close is not always advantageous for the contractor. Full audit may be preferable when:

  • There are contested costs. If you have a significant cost that DCAA might question, you want the formal audit record and the right to rebut findings and appeal to a board of contract appeals.
  • Rates are significantly above billing rates. If your actual rates exceeded provisional billing rates, a full audit may result in larger recoveries than a quick-close negotiation.
  • CAS issues exist. Any pending CAS noncompliance matters should be resolved before, not within, a quick-close.
  • The contractor is large. Large contractors with complex structures rarely qualify for quick-close and the ACO/DCAA will not agree regardless.

Summary​

Quick-close under FAR 42.708 is a practical tool for smaller contractors to achieve rate finality in months rather than years. The trade-off is waiving the right to later claims. Contractors should evaluate their cost structure, pending issues, and billing rate accuracy before requesting quick-close. When the conditions are right and the risks are low, quick-close is one of the most effective ways to reduce the administrative tail on government contract performance.
