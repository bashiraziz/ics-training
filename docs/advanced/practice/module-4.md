<Step moduleId="module4" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Navigate the Adequacy Review">
  <p>
    Cornerstone Systems filed its ICS for Fiscal Year 3 on June 28 — two days before the June 30 deadline. Forty-five days later, DCAA issues a deficiency letter identifying the following problems:
  </p>
  <p>
    (1) Schedule H (indirect cost pool summary) is present but does not tie to the general ledger — a $47,000 unreconciled difference.<br/>
    (2) The certification page is signed by the Controller, not by a "senior company official." DCAA questions whether the Controller qualifies.<br/>
    (3) Schedule I (contract listing) omits two T&M contracts that Cornerstone believes are not cost-type and therefore not required.
  </p>
  <Question answer="Item (1) — Schedule H reconciliation gap: This is a legitimate deficiency. The ICS must reconcile to the contractor's books of account. A $47,000 unreconciled difference means DCAA cannot verify the pool totals are accurate. Cornerstone must identify the source of the variance (likely a cost booked to the G&A account but not reflected in the ICS schedule, or vice versa), correct Schedule H, and resubmit. Item (2) — Certification by Controller: FAR 52.215-2 requires certification by a 'responsible official' with authority to bind the contractor. A Controller typically qualifies, but DCAA may want the CFO or CEO if the Controller's authority is not documented. Cornerstone should provide a brief written statement of the Controller's authority or have the CFO co-sign. Item (3) — T&M contracts: T&M contracts are generally not subject to ICS submission requirements unless they also have cost-reimbursable elements. Cornerstone should review each contract's type and provide DCAA with a written explanation of why the two contracts were excluded, citing the contract type and applicable clause. If they truly are pure T&M with no cost-reimbursable portion, the exclusion may be defensible.">
    Analyze each of the three deficiency items. Which are clearly legitimate deficiencies Cornerstone must fix? Which might be contestable? What should Cornerstone do about each?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="When Cornerstone corrects all three items and resubmits, the 60-day adequacy determination clock restarts from the date of resubmission. DCAA then has another 60 days to determine if the corrected submission is adequate. If DCAA finds it adequate, the audit proceeds. If DCAA issues a second deficiency letter, the clock resets again. This means Cornerstone's original June 28 filing date is no longer the controlling date — the audit clock effectively restarts on the date of the corrected resubmission. The 6-month filing window (which ran to June 30) is also tolled during the deficiency period, so the late resubmission does not count as a late filing.">
    After Cornerstone corrects all three deficiencies and resubmits, what happens to the adequacy determination clock? Does the original June 28 filing date still count for timeliness purposes?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module4" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Respond to an Information Request">
  <p>
    The adequacy issue is resolved. Three months later, Cornerstone receives Information Request #3 from the DCAA auditor. IR #3 requests:
  </p>
  <p>
    (a) Payroll registers and certified timesheets for all employees charging direct labor to Contract 47 during Q2 and Q3.<br/>
    (b) All expense reports and receipts for travel charges coded to Contract 47 during the full year.<br/>
    (c) The compensation policy in effect during Fiscal Year 3 and the board resolution approving executive salaries.
  </p>
  <Question answer="Best practices for IR responses: (1) Respond in writing, in full, by the stated due date. Never provide partial responses — DCAA will note an incomplete response and may use it as a basis for questioning whether records are adequate. (2) Organize documents to match the IR's structure: label each document package as responding to IR #3(a), IR #3(b), or IR #3(c). (3) Create and retain a response log: document the date submitted, the documents provided, the page count, and the method of delivery (email, secure portal, in-person). This log is your evidence if DCAA later claims a document was not provided. (4) For payroll and timesheet records, ensure that the timesheets are signed by both the employee and supervisor, as required by FAR 31.201-2 and DCAA's timekeeping standards. Unsigned or corrected-without-explanation timesheets will be flagged. (5) If any document cannot be provided by the deadline, notify DCAA proactively in writing, explain the reason, and provide a specific date by which it will be available.">
    Describe the best practices Cornerstone should follow when responding to IR #3. What documentation log should it maintain, and why does completeness matter?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="The auditor will use the payroll registers and timesheets to trace direct labor charges from the employee level through to the contract's direct labor cost. The auditor checks: (1) that every hour charged to Contract 47 matches a timesheet entry signed by the employee and approved by a supervisor; (2) that the labor rates applied match the payroll register for those employees (no rate mismatches); (3) that employees are classified consistently (an employee charging direct should not also be charging indirect in a way that suggests the same hours are being billed twice). For travel, the auditor compares expense reports to receipts and checks each expense against FAR 31.205-46 (travel costs): were actual costs within FAR per diem? Were business purposes documented? Were any personal expenses (upgrades, entertainment, non-business meals) included? The compensation policy and board resolution are used to verify that executive salary levels were formally approved — supporting the 'reasonableness' test under FAR 31.205-6.">
    What specific checks will the DCAA auditor perform on the documents requested in items (a), (b), and (c)? What common deficiencies should Cornerstone look for before submitting these documents?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module4" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Respond to the Draft Audit Report">
  <p>
    After field testing, DCAA issues a draft audit report with two findings: (1) $38,000 in questioned travel costs for charges that exceed the applicable GSA per diem rates, and (2) a management control finding (not a questioned cost) noting that Cornerstone's timesheet correction policy does not require supervisory approval of corrections — DCAA calls this a "significant deficiency."
  </p>
  <Question answer="For the travel finding ($38,000 questioned): Cornerstone should review each sampled transaction. FAR 31.205-46(a) allows actual costs up to the maximum per diem rate established by GSA, State Department, or DoD as applicable. If any charges exceeding per diem are for lodging in a high-cost area where an exception was pre-approved, document the exception. For meals, check whether the per diem applied was the correct locality rate (DCAA sometimes applies the wrong city rate). For airfare, higher-than-coach costs are unallowable unless specific circumstances apply. Cornerstone should compute the exact amount it concedes (costs clearly above applicable rates with no exception) and the amount it contests (charges DCAA may have miscalculated the applicable rate or the exception applies).">
    How should Cornerstone approach the $38,000 travel finding in its management response? What specific regulatory analysis should it perform on the sampled transactions before drafting the response?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="The timesheet correction finding is an internal control finding, not a questioned cost — so it does not directly disallow money. However, it signals to the ACO that Cornerstone's timekeeping system has a gap, which could increase scrutiny on future audits and could be used to question costs if DCAA later finds suspicious corrections. Cornerstone's management response should: (1) acknowledge the finding and describe what corrective action it is taking (e.g., immediately updating the timesheet correction policy to require supervisory approval and a written explanation for any correction); (2) provide a timeline for implementation; (3) optionally note that no questioned costs resulted from this gap, which limits its current financial impact. Implementing the corrective action before the final report is issued shows good faith and may cause DCAA to soften the finding's language in the final report.">
    What is the significance of the internal control finding, and how should Cornerstone address it in the management response even though no costs are immediately at risk?
  </Question>
</Step>
