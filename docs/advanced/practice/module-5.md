<Step moduleId="module5" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Analyze and Categorize Findings">
  <p>
    Vector Analytics received a draft audit report for Fiscal Year 2 with three findings:
  </p>
  <p>
    <strong>Finding A:</strong> $22,500 in "client appreciation" expenses (sporting event tickets, restaurant charges over $200/person) charged to G&A — DCAA says these are entertainment costs unallowable under FAR 31.205-14.<br/><br/>
    <strong>Finding B:</strong> $155,000 in questioned costs for an independent consultant who provided "strategic advisory" services. DCAA says the invoices lack a statement of work, deliverables, or evidence that services were actually received.<br/><br/>
    <strong>Finding C:</strong> A finding that Vector's accounting system does not have adequate controls to prevent direct costs from being charged to indirect accounts, citing two instances where a direct labor charge was initially coded to overhead and later journal-journaled to a contract.
  </p>
  <Question answer="Finding A is a questioned cost — specifically an unallowable cost under FAR 31.205-14. Entertainment is expressly unallowable. Sporting event tickets and high-cost restaurant charges for clients are classic entertainment. This finding is difficult to rebut if the expenses clearly were entertainment. The only avenue: determine if any events had a documented business purpose (e.g., a business discussion took place) and whether any portion could be reclassified as allowable business promotion under FAR 31.205-1. Finding B is a questioned cost — inadequately supported. DCAA is not saying consulting is unallowable; it is saying Vector cannot prove the services were received. Finding C is an internal control finding — no costs are disallowed now, but the finding signals a system weakness. Categorizing correctly matters because the response strategy differs: A requires a regulatory defense or concession; B requires documentation; C requires a corrective action plan.">
    Categorize each finding (questioned cost — unallowable, questioned cost — inadequately supported, or internal control finding). Explain why categorization matters for the response strategy.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Vector should concede Finding A in full ($22,500). Sporting event tickets and restaurant charges over $200/person for clients are entertainment — expressly unallowable under FAR 31.205-14 with no exception that applies. Attempting to defend these will undermine credibility on the more defensible Finding B. A clean concession on Finding A, paired with a strong defense of Finding B, is the right approach. For Finding C, Vector should implement immediate corrective action (enhanced journal entry review, dual approval for re-coding direct costs to indirect accounts) and document it in the response. Conceding the small, indefensible finding while vigorously defending the larger finding is a common and effective negotiation strategy.">
    Should Vector concede any findings immediately? Which one, and why is early concession sometimes strategically better than contesting everything?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module5" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Build the Response to Finding B">
  <p>
    For Finding B, Vector's management believes the $155,000 consulting engagement was legitimate. The consultant, Dr. Sarah Chen, provided market analysis and technology roadmap advice that informed Vector's IR&D strategy. Vector has: (1) the original consulting agreement (signed, showing $155,000 total value and a general scope); (2) 12 monthly invoices; (3) emails between Dr. Chen and Vector's CTO discussing analytical findings; (4) a final PowerPoint presentation Dr. Chen delivered at a board offsite. No formal deliverables were specified in the contract and no formal deliverable acceptance records exist.
  </p>
  <Question answer="Under FAR 31.205-33 (professional and consultant service costs), consulting costs are allowable when: (1) the nature and scope of services are necessary and reasonable for contract performance or business operations; (2) the services are actually performed; (3) compensation is reasonable; and (4) the costs are properly supported. The critical question for Finding B is (2) and (4). Vector has circumstantial evidence that services were performed (emails, final presentation, monthly invoices) but lacks formal deliverable acceptance records. The response should: cite FAR 31.205-33; provide the consulting agreement, all 12 invoices, the email chain showing substantive analytical work, and the PowerPoint presentation; explain the business purpose (IR&D strategy); show that the monthly invoices reflect ongoing work. The absence of formal deliverable records is a weakness, but the email correspondence and final presentation constitute contemporaneous evidence that work was actually performed.">
    Draft the regulatory framework for Vector's management response to Finding B. What FAR provision governs consulting costs, and what elements must Vector demonstrate? What evidence it has is strongest, and what gap exists?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Vector can strengthen its position by: (1) Obtaining a signed statement from Dr. Chen confirming the services she provided and the dates of performance — this is a third-party attestation that work was done. (2) Obtaining a statement from Vector's CTO describing the meetings, discussions, and how Dr. Chen's analysis influenced specific IR&D decisions — this ties the cost to a business benefit. (3) If any IR&D projects resulted from Dr. Chen's work, documenting that connection. (4) For the argument on reasonableness, obtaining market data on comparable consulting rates (e.g., $155,000 / 12 months = ~$12,900/month for a senior technology strategist — compare to published consulting rate benchmarks). None of these are perfect substitutes for contemporaneous deliverable records, but together they constitute a reasonable body of evidence that the services were performed and the cost was reasonable.">
    What additional evidence can Vector gather or create after the fact to strengthen its response? Be specific about what documents or statements would be most persuasive to DCAA and the ACO.
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module5" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Handle the ACO Negotiation">
  <p>
    DCAA issues its final audit report maintaining all three findings. The ACO schedules a negotiation conference. Before the conference, the ACO sends a position paper indicating she is prepared to disallow Finding A in full ($22,500), disallow 75% of Finding B ($116,250), and require a corrective action plan for Finding C within 60 days.
  </p>
  <Question answer="The ACO's position on Finding B (75% disallowance) reflects a compromise — she is not fully accepting DCAA's position but is not fully accepting Vector's defense either. This creates a negotiating opening. Vector should accept the ACO's position on Finding A (Vector already conceded this). For Finding B, Vector should present its full evidentiary package at the negotiation conference: the consulting agreement, emails, CTO statement, Dr. Chen's confirmation, the PowerPoint, and the market rate analysis. The goal is to reduce the disallowance further — perhaps to 25-30% (representing only the months where corroborating evidence is thinnest). Vector should come to the conference with a specific counter-proposal (e.g., 'We propose a $35,000 disallowance representing the months where we acknowledge documentation is incomplete, and we defend the remaining $120,000 as fully supported.'). For Finding C, Vector should provide the corrective action plan proactively before the conference — this demonstrates responsiveness and may cause the ACO to reduce any interest or ongoing concern.">
    How should Vector approach the ACO negotiation conference? What position should it take on each finding, and what is the realistic negotiating range for Finding B?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="If the ACO issues a COFD disallowing $138,750 ($22,500 + $116,250), Vector has 90 days from receipt to appeal to the Armed Services Board of Contract Appeals (ASBCA) or the relevant board, or 12 months to file in the Court of Federal Claims. Before deciding whether to appeal, Vector should do a cost-benefit analysis: legal fees for a Board of Contract Appeals case typically run $50,000-$150,000+ depending on complexity. The amount in dispute (after accepting Finding A) is $116,250 in Finding B disallowances. If Vector believes it can recover 50% ($58,125) through appeal, the expected recovery roughly equals minimum legal fees — making appeal marginal. However, if DCAA's position would be applied to future years (i.e., if this is a recurring consulting arrangement), winning the appeal creates precedent that protects Vector in future audits. That systemic value can make an appeal worthwhile even when the immediate dollar recovery is borderline.">
    If the ACO issues a COFD that Vector disagrees with, what are Vector's appeal options and deadlines? Walk through the cost-benefit analysis of whether to appeal given the amounts involved.
  </Question>
</Step>
