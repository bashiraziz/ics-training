## What Is a DCAA Finding?

A DCAA finding is a formal conclusion in the audit report that one or more costs are questioned, that an internal control weakness exists, or that the contractor is not in compliance with CAS. Findings carry financial weight: they are the basis for the ACO's decisions on final rates and contract adjustments. Understanding how to respond effectively can mean the difference between absorbing a six-figure disallowance and successfully defending your costs.

## Types of Findings

**Questioned costs** are the most common finding type. DCAA questions costs that it believes are:
- **Unallowable under FAR 31.2** — explicitly unallowable categories such as entertainment, fines and penalties, and certain lobbying costs.
- **Unreasonable** — costs that exceed what a prudent businessperson would incur, such as executive compensation significantly above published benchmarks.
- **Inadequately supported** — costs for which the contractor cannot produce sufficient documentation (receipts, contracts, timesheets).

**Internal control findings** identify weaknesses in the contractor's accounting system — for example, inadequate segregation of duties, inability to trace costs from general ledger to invoices, or inconsistent timekeeping practices. These findings do not themselves disallow costs but signal to the ACO that additional scrutiny is warranted.

**CAS noncompliance findings** arise when the contractor's actual cost accounting practices differ from what is described in its Disclosure Statement or required by a specific CAS standard. These findings trigger mandatory price adjustments on CAS-covered contracts.

## The Draft Report Response: Your Most Important Opportunity

When DCAA issues the draft audit report, the contractor has the right — and strategically, the obligation — to submit a **written management response**. This is your best opportunity to influence the final report before it reaches the ACO.

A strong management response:

1. **Addresses each finding individually.** Number your responses to correspond to the draft report's finding numbers. Do not lump multiple findings into one response.
2. **Cites the applicable regulation.** If DCAA questions travel costs as unreasonable, cite FAR 31.205-46 and explain how your travel policy and actual costs comply.
3. **Provides documentary evidence.** Attach the receipts, the compensation survey, the subcontract, or whatever document supports your position. Don't just assert your costs are allowable — prove it.
4. **Quantifies your position.** If you agree that $15,000 should be disallowed but disagree on the remaining $85,000, state that clearly. Partial agreement shows good faith and focuses the dispute on the real disagreement.
5. **Proposes an alternative.** If DCAA's benchmark for executive compensation is a particular survey, but you believe a different survey is more applicable, explain why and provide both.

## Common Findings and How to Rebut Them

**Entertainment costs (FAR 31.205-14).** Entertainment is expressly unallowable. However, costs that appear to be entertainment may actually be allowable business promotion or employee morale costs. Document the business purpose of every expense: who attended, what business was discussed, the relationship to government contract performance. A meal at a restaurant with a government client to discuss contract performance is potentially allowable business promotion; a holiday party open to all employees may qualify as an allowable morale cost under FAR 31.205-13 with proper documentation.

**Executive compensation (FAR 31.205-6(p)).** Compensation is allowable to the extent it is reasonable. DCAA benchmarks executive pay against published surveys (often Radford, Mercer, or Willis Towers Watson). To rebut: (a) confirm DCAA used the correct peer group (company size, industry, geography), (b) provide a current compensation survey from a recognized source, (c) document the executive's unique qualifications or special circumstances. Even if DCAA's benchmark is correct, compensation within a reasonable range around the benchmark should be defensible.

**Inadequately supported costs.** When documentation is missing, your first response is to find it. Check archives, email systems, credit card statements, and third-party vendor records. If a document is genuinely lost, provide a written explanation of what the cost was, why it was incurred, and what substitute evidence exists (e.g., a canceled check plus a vendor invoice). DCAA has some discretion to accept reconstructed documentation; the ACO has more.

## After the Final Report: The ACO's Role

Once the final audit report is issued, the ACO takes over. The ACO may:

- Accept DCAA's findings and issue a **Contracting Officer Final Decision (COFD)** disallowing the questioned costs.
- Negotiate with the contractor and agree on a different outcome.
- Reject DCAA's position if the ACO finds the contractor's rebuttal persuasive.

Do not assume that DCAA's final report is the end. The ACO has independent authority. Engage the ACO directly — request a negotiation conference, provide your position paper, and make your case.

## The COFD and Appeal Rights

If the ACO issues a COFD disallowing costs, the contractor has formal appeal rights under the Contract Disputes Act:

- **Board of Contract Appeals (ASBCA or other applicable board):** Appeal must be filed within **90 days** of receiving the COFD.
- **Court of Federal Claims:** Lawsuit must be filed within **12 months** of receiving the COFD.

Appeals are formal legal proceedings. Engage legal counsel experienced in government contracts before filing. The cost-benefit analysis matters: an appeal over $15,000 in disallowed costs rarely makes economic sense given legal fees. An appeal over $500,000 with strong supporting evidence is worth evaluating seriously.

## When to Accept vs. Contest

Not every finding is worth fighting. Consider accepting a finding when:
- The regulatory basis for disallowance is clear and your documentation is weak.
- The disallowed amount is small relative to the cost of contesting.
- Acceptance on a minor point strengthens your credibility on major contested items.

Contest a finding when:
- You have strong documentary evidence and a clear regulatory argument.
- The amount is material.
- The finding reflects a misunderstanding of your accounting practice that, if left uncorrected, will recur in future audit years.

## Summary

Responding to DCAA findings is both a regulatory process and a negotiation. The draft report response is your primary tool; use it methodically, cite regulations precisely, and back every position with evidence. Engage the ACO as a decision-maker separate from DCAA. And if a COFD issues, know your appeal deadlines — missing them waives your rights permanently.
