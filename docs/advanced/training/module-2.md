## What Is the CAS Disclosure Statement?

The Cost Accounting Standards (CAS) Disclosure Statement — filed on Form CASB DS-1 (for educational institutions, DS-2) — is a contractor's written description of its cost accounting practices. Think of it as a rulebook the contractor publishes about how it accumulates, classifies, and allocates costs. Once filed, the contractor is bound by those practices.

The Disclosure Statement matters for ICS purposes because **DCAA will compare your actual ICS to your Disclosure Statement during audit**. If you said you allocate G&A on Total Cost Input (TCI), but your ICS uses Value Added, that is a CAS noncompliance — regardless of whether the alternative method might be more accurate.

## What Triggers CAS Coverage?

CAS coverage is triggered by the type and size of contracts awarded:

| Situation | Coverage Level |
|---|---|
| First CAS-covered contract ≥ $2M (but < $50M CAS-covered in prior year) | **Modified CAS coverage** — CAS 401, 402, 405, 406 only |
| CAS-covered awards exceed $50M in prior cost accounting period | **Full CAS coverage** — all applicable CAS standards |
| Educational institutions and certain contracts | Special rules apply |

Contracts that are exempt from CAS include firm-fixed-price contracts awarded without submission of cost or pricing data, contracts below the simplified acquisition threshold, and contracts with small businesses. Once you cross the $50M threshold, full CAS applies and a Disclosure Statement must be filed (and maintained) for each segment.

## Key Disclosure Statement Sections That Affect ICS

The DS-1 has eight sections. Three are directly relevant to your ICS:

**Section 3 — Indirect Costs.** This section describes how indirect costs are pooled and allocated. It specifies:
- What costs go into each indirect cost pool (overhead, G&A, fringe, etc.)
- The allocation base for each pool (direct labor dollars, direct labor hours, total cost input, value added, etc.)
- Any special allocation methods

**Section 4 — Depreciation and Capital Expenditures.** Describes how capital assets are depreciated and whether depreciation rates differ from book to cost accounting. ICS Schedule B must align with the disclosed depreciation methodology.

**Section 6 — Deferred Compensation and Insurance.** Describes how pension costs and insurance premiums are measured. ICS must use the same measurement basis disclosed here — for example, if the DS says pension costs are measured on the accrual method under CAS 412, the ICS cannot substitute a cash-payment figure.

## What DCAA Looks for in the DS-ICS Comparison

During adequacy review and the desk audit phase, DCAA auditors pull the contractor's current Disclosure Statement and compare it section-by-section to the ICS. Common discrepancies they flag:

1. **Allocation base mismatch.** DS says TCI, ICS uses a modified TCI that excludes certain costs the DS doesn't exclude.
2. **Pool composition changes.** A cost category moved from overhead to G&A without an approved DS amendment.
3. **New cost elements.** The contractor began incurring a new category of cost (e.g., stock-based compensation) after the DS was filed but did not amend to describe the accounting treatment.
4. **Subsidiary or segment inconsistency.** A segment's ICS practices differ from another segment's, and the DS does not disclose the difference as an intentional segment-level practice.

Any discrepancy DCAA identifies goes into the audit report as a **CAS noncompliance finding**. This triggers a price adjustment for all affected CAS-covered contracts, calculated as the difference between costs computed under the disclosed practice and costs as actually incurred.

## How to Handle Disclosure Statement Amendments

When your accounting practices change — due to system upgrades, corporate restructuring, or a deliberate improvement — you must amend the Disclosure Statement **before** implementing the change (for "required" changes) or within 60 days of implementation (for "unilateral" changes, which require retroactive price adjustments).

The process:
1. **Identify the change.** Compare your current practice to what the DS describes. If they diverge, an amendment is needed.
2. **Notify the ACO.** Submit a written notice with the proposed change and its effective date.
3. **File the amended DS.** Submit the revised DS pages to DCAA and the ACO.
4. **Compute the impact.** Calculate the cost impact on existing CAS-covered contracts.
5. **Agree on the adjustment.** If the new practice increases costs, the contractor pays the difference. If it decreases costs, the government is owed a credit.

Proactively amending the DS is far less costly than having DCAA discover an undisclosed change during audit — which results in a noncompliance finding, potential penalties, and retroactive adjustments covering multiple ICS years.

## Practical DS-ICS Alignment Checklist

Before submitting your ICS, run through this checklist:

- [ ] Pull the current approved Disclosure Statement for each segment.
- [ ] Verify your ICS allocation bases match what Section 3 describes.
- [ ] Confirm that pool compositions have not changed from what is disclosed.
- [ ] Check that any new cost elements have been addressed (either described in the existing DS or an amendment has been filed).
- [ ] Reconcile depreciation in ICS Schedule B to the Section 4 method.
- [ ] Confirm pension/insurance amounts in the ICS match Section 6 treatment.
- [ ] If practices changed mid-year, verify the DS amendment was filed timely and the ICS reflects the correct split between pre-change and post-change periods.

## Summary

The CAS Disclosure Statement is the contract between the contractor and the government about how costs are accounted for. Your ICS is the proof that you honored that contract during the fiscal year. Keeping the two aligned is not just a compliance formality — it protects you from noncompliance findings that trigger retroactive cost adjustments across every CAS-covered contract in your portfolio.
