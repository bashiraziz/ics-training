## Why M&A Complicates ICS

Open ICS years are contingent liabilities. They represent fiscal years where indirect cost rates have not been finalized, meaning the contractor's cost-type contracts still have unsettled billings. When a company is acquired or merges with another, those open years — and the potential disallowances embedded in them — transfer to the new owner. Buyers who do not adequately evaluate ICS exposure during due diligence can find themselves inheriting millions of dollars in government claims.

## Novation Agreements: FAR 42.1204

When a government contractor's assets (or the contractor itself) are transferred to a successor in interest — through a sale, merger, or reorganization — the government must formally recognize the successor as the new contract party through a **novation agreement** under FAR 42.1204.

Key points about novation:

- The government is **not required** to novate; it evaluates whether recognizing the successor is in the government's interest.
- Until novation is approved, the original contractor remains the contract party of record. Performing work on government contracts after an asset sale without a novation agreement in place creates legal exposure for both the old and new entity.
- The novation agreement explicitly transfers responsibility for all contract obligations — including **open ICS years** — to the successor.
- The successor takes on the obligation to file ICS for years already completed, to cooperate with DCAA audits of those years, and to repay any amounts disallowed as a result.

The novation package submitted to the government must include financial statements demonstrating the successor's ability to perform, legal documents establishing the transfer of assets, and a list of all affected contracts.

## Due Diligence: ICS-Specific Items

Before signing a purchase agreement, an acquirer should conduct ICS-specific due diligence:

**1. How many ICS years are open?**
Count the number of fiscal years where final indirect rates have not been established. Each open year represents an unresolved contingency. Two or three open years is common; seven or eight open years suggests the seller has not been managing its government contract compliance obligations.

**2. What are the known audit findings?**
Request copies of all DCAA audit reports, management responses, and COFD letters for the last ten years. Known findings that were not fully resolved may re-emerge post-acquisition.

**3. What are the provisional billing rates?**
Compare provisional billing rates to actual cost data for each open year. A large variance suggests either over-billing (a liability) or under-billing (an asset). Both need to be quantified.

**4. Are there any unallowable cost categories in the historical G&A or overhead?**
Review expense ledgers for categories commonly questioned by DCAA: entertainment, lobbying costs, IR&D in excess of negotiated limits, excessive compensation. Even if these costs were not yet questioned, they may be challenged during the post-acquisition audit.

**5. Is the CAS Disclosure Statement current?**
A DS that is out of date or inconsistent with actual practices is a noncompliance waiting to be found. Post-acquisition, the buyer may also need to file DS amendments reflecting the combined entity's cost accounting practices.

## Purchase Price Adjustments and Escrow Holdbacks

Given the uncertainty around open ICS years, acquisition agreements for government contractors typically include:

- **Escrow holdbacks:** A portion of the purchase price (often 5-15% for companies with multiple open ICS years) is held in escrow for 3-5 years pending resolution of DCAA audit findings. Released to the seller as years close without material disallowance.
- **Indemnification clauses:** The seller (or selling shareholders) indemnify the buyer against disallowances exceeding a specified threshold in the open ICS years. The indemnity period mirrors the potential audit window — often 7-10 years.
- **Reps and warranties:** Seller makes representations about the accuracy of its ICS filings, the absence of known unallowable costs, and the current status of any DCAA audits.

## CAS Disclosure Statement Amendments Triggered by M&A

Corporate restructuring almost always requires DS amendments because:

- The combined entity's cost accounting practices may differ from either predecessor's disclosed practices.
- Segment definitions change when businesses are combined or divided.
- New cost pools may be created or eliminated.
- The allocation bases may change as the combined workforce and cost structure evolves.

Failure to file required DS amendments is itself a CAS noncompliance. The acquirer should conduct a DS gap analysis within 90 days of acquisition close and file amendments as needed.

## Segment Divestitures

When a segment is sold off rather than acquired, the remaining contractor must unwind any ICS submissions that combined the divested segment's costs with other segments. This involves:

- Allocating historical home office costs between the divested and retained segments.
- Determining which contracts transfer with the divested segment and which stay.
- Filing amended ICS submissions if the original submissions combined segments that must now be separated.
- Negotiating with the ACO about how to handle open years that include both segments' costs.

## Sample Scenario: Acme Federal Services Acquisition

**Situation:** TechCorp acquires Acme Federal Services, a 300-person government contractor, for $45M. Acme has three open ICS years (Year 1, Year 2, Year 3). DCAA has issued a draft audit report for Year 1 with $280,000 in questioned costs.

**Due diligence findings:**
- Year 1 draft audit questioned $280,000. Acme's management response is pending.
- Year 2 has been submitted but adequacy is unresolved (deficiency letter issued 4 months ago).
- Year 3 has not yet been filed (due in 2 months).
- CAS DS was last updated 6 years ago and does not reflect Acme's current G&A allocation base (changed 3 years ago — unaddressed noncompliance).

**Risk quantification:**
- Year 1: $280,000 questioned, plus potential interest. Escrow $350,000.
- Year 2: Unknown until audit. Conservative reserve $200,000.
- Year 3: Not yet filed; TechCorp will be responsible for filing on time.
- CAS noncompliance: Retroactive price adjustments on all CAS-covered contracts for the 3-year period of non-disclosure. Potentially $150,000-$400,000 depending on audit outcome.

TechCorp negotiates an escrow holdback of $750,000 and a seller indemnity for disallowances exceeding $100,000 in Years 1 and 2.

## Summary

ICS exposure in M&A is a genuine financial risk that must be quantified and priced into the deal. Novation transfers open year obligations to the successor; due diligence must uncover the depth of that exposure before close. Escrow holdbacks, indemnification clauses, and DS gap analyses are the standard tools for managing this risk. Acquirers who understand the ICS lifecycle are better positioned to negotiate appropriate deal protections.
