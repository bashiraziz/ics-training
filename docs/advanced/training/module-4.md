## Overview of the ICS Audit Lifecycle

After you submit your Incurred Cost Submission, the audit process begins — but it unfolds over years, not weeks. Understanding each phase helps you prepare the right documentation, respond promptly, and avoid unnecessary delays that can stretch an already slow process even further.

The eight phases of a DCAA ICS audit are: adequacy review, desk review, sampling and risk assessment, document requests, field audit, draft report, final report, and rate negotiation/agreement. Each phase has its own timeline, deliverables, and risk points.

## Phase 1: Adequacy Review (Target: 60 Days)

Within 60 days of receiving your ICS, DCAA must determine whether the submission is **adequate** — meaning it is complete enough to begin a meaningful audit. DCAA's adequacy checklist (based on FAR 52.215-2 and DCAA guidance) covers items such as:

- All required schedules present (A through O)
- Indirect cost pools and allocation bases identified
- Reconciliation to financial statements included
- Certification signed by an authorized official

If the submission is deemed **inadequate**, DCAA issues a deficiency letter identifying missing items. The 6-month statutory filing window pauses during this period. Once you correct and resubmit, the 60-day adequacy clock restarts. Repeated inadequacy determinations can delay your ICS year for years before audit even begins.

If the submission is **adequate**, DCAA notifies you and moves to desk review.

## Phase 2: Desk Review

During the desk review, the assigned DCAA auditor reviews the ICS without visiting your office. This review covers:

- Mathematical accuracy of rate calculations
- Internal consistency (do the schedules cross-foot correctly?)
- Year-over-year rate fluctuations that suggest unusual cost behavior
- Comparison of actual rates to provisional billing rates
- A first pass at potential unallowable cost areas

The auditor may issue an **Information Request (IR)** — a written list of questions and document requests. Respond promptly and in writing. Each IR response should be complete; partial responses slow the process and may lead the auditor to conclude records are inadequate.

## Phase 3: Sampling and Risk Assessment

DCAA does not audit every dollar on every contract. It uses a **risk-based sampling** approach. The auditor identifies:

- **High-risk cost elements:** Travel, consultant fees, entertainment, compensation, bonuses, independent R&D.
- **High-value contracts:** Contracts with the largest direct cost dollars are sampled with greater probability.
- **Prior year findings:** If a prior year's audit found questioned costs in a specific category, that category will be tested more heavily.

The sampling methodology is documented in the audit program. Contractors typically do not see the audit program directly, but you can infer what will be tested based on information requests.

## Phase 4: Document Requests and Field Testing

This is the most labor-intensive phase for contractors. DCAA will request:

- Payroll registers and timesheets for sampled employees
- Invoices and receipts for sampled cost elements
- Subcontract agreements and subcontractor invoices
- Board minutes, expense reports, and compensation benchmarking studies
- Bank statements, general ledger detail, and journal entries

Organize your document room or secure portal carefully. Provide only what is requested — do not volunteer information that was not asked for. Maintain a log of every document provided, including the date, document description, and the information request it responds to. This log is your protection if DCAA later claims a document was not provided.

Field visits may include interviews with accounting staff, program managers, or HR personnel. Prepare these individuals by reviewing what is likely to be asked, but do not coach them to give misleading answers.

## Phase 5: Draft Audit Report

After fieldwork is complete, DCAA prepares a **draft audit report**. This report identifies:

- Questioned costs by category and amount
- Internal control findings or deficiencies
- CAS noncompliance findings
- Any cost elements DCAA found allowable and reasonable (the "passed" items)

The contractor receives the draft report and has the opportunity to submit a **management response** — a written rebuttal to findings you disagree with. This is a critical opportunity. A strong management response that provides regulatory citations and documentary evidence can cause DCAA to reduce or withdraw a finding before the final report is issued.

The management response period is typically 30 days but can be extended by agreement.

## Phase 6: Final Audit Report

After considering the contractor's management response, DCAA issues the **final audit report** to the ACO. The final report may:

- Maintain all findings from the draft
- Withdraw or reduce findings based on your response
- Add clarifications or additional context

Once issued, the final audit report is forwarded to the ACO, who uses it as the basis for rate negotiation.

## Phase 7: Rate Negotiation with the ACO

The ACO — not DCAA — has the authority to establish final indirect cost rates. The final audit report is DCAA's recommendation; the ACO can accept it, reject it, or negotiate a different outcome.

Rate negotiation typically involves:
- A negotiation conference where both sides present positions
- Exchange of position papers on contested cost elements
- Possible referral back to DCAA for additional analysis of specific items

Negotiations may conclude quickly (if the audit had few findings) or extend for months if there are significant disputed costs.

## Phase 8: Final Rate Agreement

The ACO issues a **final indirect cost rate agreement** (FICRA) that establishes the definitive rates for every indirect cost pool for the year. This document closes the ICS year. With final rates established:

- Contractors submit final invoices on cost-type contracts
- Overbilled amounts are repaid with interest
- Underbilled amounts are collected
- Contracts eligible for closeout can proceed to formal closeout

## Typical Elapsed Time by Phase

| Phase | Typical Duration |
|---|---|
| Adequacy review | 1-3 months |
| Desk review | 3-12 months |
| Document requests / field testing | 6-18 months |
| Draft report issuance | 3-6 months after fieldwork |
| Management response period | 1-2 months |
| Final report issuance | 1-3 months after response |
| Rate negotiation | 3-12 months |
| **Total: submission to final rates** | **3-7 years typical** |

## Summary

The DCAA ICS audit is a long, multi-phase process. Your most effective tool is preparation: a well-organized ICS, clean documentation, and prompt responses to information requests. Each delay you introduce — a slow response to an adequacy deficiency letter, missing invoices, incomplete IR responses — adds months to an already lengthy timeline. Contractors who treat DCAA as a partner in the process (providing complete, organized information promptly) generally fare better than those who are adversarial or slow to respond.
