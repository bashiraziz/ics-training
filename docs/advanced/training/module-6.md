## Why Limitations Periods Matter in ICS

Government contract cost disputes are governed by the **Contract Disputes Act (CDA), 41 U.S.C. §§ 7101-7109**. The CDA sets deadlines for both contractors and the government to assert claims. Miss a deadline and you permanently lose the right to pursue that claim — no exceptions. For ICS purposes, this means understanding when the clock starts, how long it runs, and what actions preserve or forfeit your rights.

## The Six-Year Claim Rule

Both the contractor and the government have **six years** from the date a claim accrues to submit that claim to the contracting officer. This is not a soft guideline — it is a jurisdictional requirement. Claims filed after six years are barred, regardless of their merit.

**What does "accrual" mean?**

A claim accrues when all events that fix the alleged liability have occurred and the claimant knew or should have known of the claim. For ICS-related disputes:

- A **government claim** for disallowed costs generally accrues when the costs are incurred and included in an ICS — typically the date the ICS was submitted or the date final rates were established, depending on the nature of the dispute.
- A **contractor claim** for underbilled amounts accrues when final rates are established (because that is when the contractor knows with certainty that it was underpaid).

The practical implication: if your ICS year remains open and unaudited for many years, the six-year window may still be running — but for which party and on which costs is a fact-intensive question. Leaving ICS years open indefinitely is not a safe strategy for either side.

## The Contracting Officer Final Decision Timeline

When a contractor submits a **certified claim** to the contracting officer, the CDA requires the CO to issue a **Contracting Officer Final Decision (COFD)** within:

- **60 days** for claims of **$100,000 or less** (or within a reasonable time).
- **60 days** for claims over $100,000 — but within that 60-day window, the CO may instead provide a written notice of when a decision will be issued, so long as it is within a reasonable time.

Failure to issue a COFD within the required period constitutes a **deemed denial**, which the contractor may immediately appeal to a Board of Contract Appeals or the Court of Federal Claims.

## Contractor Claim Certification Requirements

For any contractor claim exceeding **$100,000**, the claim must be certified. The certification, signed by a senior company official, states:

1. The claim is made in good faith.
2. The supporting data are accurate and complete to the best of the official's knowledge.
3. The amount requested accurately reflects the contract adjustment for which the contractor believes the government is liable.

An improperly certified or uncertified claim over $100,000 does not toll the CO's decision deadline and may be dismissed. Get the certification right the first time.

## Appeal Deadlines After a COFD

Once the CO issues a COFD, the contractor faces strict appeal windows:

| Forum | Deadline |
|---|---|
| Board of Contract Appeals (ASBCA or CBCA) | **90 days** from receipt of COFD |
| U.S. Court of Federal Claims | **12 months** from receipt of COFD |

These deadlines are jurisdictional — courts and boards do not have authority to hear late-filed appeals, regardless of the reason for the delay. Calendar these dates immediately upon receiving a COFD.

## CDA Interest Provisions

The CDA provides that interest accrues on contractor claims from the date the claim is submitted to the CO until the government pays. The interest rate is the U.S. Treasury rate established under the Renegotiation Act, adjusted semi-annually. This interest provision matters because:

- On a large underbilling claim, interest over several years can be substantial.
- For government claims against the contractor, the government is also entitled to interest — meaning unresolved overpayments accrue interest against the contractor.

Prompt resolution of ICS years benefits both parties by stopping the interest clock.

## Consequences of Letting ICS Years Stay Open

Contractors sometimes allow ICS years to remain open — neither quick-closed nor fully audited — for a decade or more. This creates serious risks:

**Document retention.** The FAR generally requires contractors to retain records for three years after final payment on the last contract covered by that fiscal year. If an ICS year is never closed, there is no "final payment" to start that clock. DCAA has successfully argued that records must be retained for open ICS years indefinitely. If records are lost or destroyed, DCAA may question all costs in categories where it cannot verify documentation.

**Expanding audit risk.** An open ICS year is always subject to audit. While the CDA six-year rule limits the government's ability to assert new claims, DCAA's audit authority for adequately submitted ICS years does not necessarily expire. The government may assert claims within six years of when specific cost items accrued — meaning the government's claim window may be refreshed by the discovery of specific unallowable costs during a late audit.

**Contractor claim risk.** The contractor's own right to pursue underbilling claims may be extinguished if the six-year window closes before final rates are established and a claim is submitted.

**Business disruption.** Mergers, acquisitions, and contract closeouts are all blocked or complicated by open ICS years. Buyers discount acquisition prices for unresolved ICS liabilities.

## Practical Strategy: Don't Let Years Pile Up

The best approach is to close ICS years promptly:
- File the ICS on time (within 6 months of fiscal year end).
- Respond promptly to adequacy deficiency letters.
- Pursue quick-close for years that qualify.
- Engage the ACO proactively if a year is aging without audit activity.
- Document any agreements to toll deadlines or extend time limits.

## Summary

The CDA's six-year rule, the 60-day COFD requirement, and the 90-day/12-month appeal windows are hard deadlines with no grace periods. For contractors, the most dangerous risk is not any single finding — it is allowing ICS years to accumulate unresolved until document retention problems, statute of limitations questions, and compounding interest make an already complex situation unmanageable. Close your ICS years promptly and calendar every deadline.
