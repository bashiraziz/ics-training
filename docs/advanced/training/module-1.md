## What Is a Segment?

Under CAS 403, a **segment** is one of two or more divisions, product departments, plants, or other subdivisions of an organization that reports to a common home office and has separately identified costs. The key word is "separately identified" — each segment maintains its own books, uses its own indirect cost pools, and operates with a distinct allocation base.

Large government contractors often have two, five, or even twenty segments. Each segment files its own Incurred Cost Submission covering only that segment's direct contracts and indirect cost structure. The home office files a separate ICS covering centralized costs that are then allocated down to the segments.

## Why Segments Matter for ICS

When a contractor operates as a single entity, one ICS covers everything. When segments enter the picture, the filing obligation multiplies — and so do the compliance risks. DCAA and the ACO must verify that:

1. Each segment's indirect rates reflect only that segment's costs and applicable home office allocations.
2. Inter-segment transactions are properly handled (not double-counted).
3. The home office allocation pools are accurately reflected in each segment's G&A or overhead pool.

Failure on any of these points can result in questioned costs or a CAS noncompliance finding.

## Home Office Cost Allocation (CAS 403)

The home office is the corporate center that provides services to the segments. Under CAS 403, home office costs must be allocated using one of three methods:

| Method | When Used |
|---|---|
| **Direct allocation** | Cost benefits only one segment (e.g., a legal settlement specific to one division) |
| **Residual allocation** | Remaining home office costs allocated using a three-factor formula (payroll, revenue, assets) or other equitable base |
| **Special allocation** | Certain costs (e.g., independent R&D managed centrally) allocated by specific criteria |

The home office ICS must show: total home office costs, the allocation methodology, and how much each segment received. Each segment's ICS must then include that home office allocation in its own indirect cost pools — typically in G&A.

## Two-Segment Example: Apex Defense Group

Consider **Apex Defense Group**, which has:

- **Segment A — Systems Integration** (200 employees, $60M revenue)
- **Segment B — Professional Services** (150 employees, $35M revenue)
- **Home Office** — HR, Finance, Legal, and executive leadership ($8M annual costs)

**Home office allocation** uses the three-factor formula:

| Factor | Segment A | Segment B | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Payroll | $12M | $7M | $19M |
| Revenue | $60M | $35M | $95M |
| Assets | $4M | $2M | $6M |
| Composite weight | 63% | 37% | 100% |

Home office costs of $8M are split: $5.04M to Segment A, $2.96M to Segment B. Each segment's G&A pool includes this allocated amount as an identifiable line item in its own ICS Schedule H.

## Inter-Segment Transactions

When Segment A provides engineering support to Segment B on a contract, this is an **inter-segment transaction**. Two rules apply:

1. **Pricing rule (CAS 414 / FAR 31.205-26):** The transfer price is generally the transferring segment's cost, not a market price, unless the segment routinely sells the same service commercially.
2. **Elimination rule:** The receiving segment must not include the inter-segment transfer in its allocation base in a way that inflates costs double-counted at the home office level.

DCAA will cross-check both segments' ICS submissions to verify that inter-segment flows net to zero at the consolidated level.

## Filing Logistics for Multi-Segment Contractors

Each segment files its ICS **by segment**, submitted to the cognizant DCAA field audit office for that segment. If segments are geographically dispersed, different DCAA offices may cover them. The home office ICS is submitted to the DCAA office cognizant of the home office.

Key coordination points:
- **Consistent fiscal year:** All segment ICS filings cover the same contractor fiscal year.
- **Consistent definitions:** "Direct labor," "fringe," and "G&A" must be defined the same way across segments (CAS 401 consistency requirement).
- **Timely home office filing:** Segments cannot finalize their G&A rates until the home office allocation is known; the home office ICS should be filed first or simultaneously.

## Common Errors in Multi-Segment Filings

**Double-counting home office costs.** A segment inadvertently includes home office costs in its own G&A pool AND in its allocation base, inflating rates.

**Misclassifying inter-segment work as subcontract costs.** Subcontract costs are typically excluded from the G&A base (under a Value-Added G&A structure), but inter-segment work may be included depending on the segment's disclosed practice.

**Inconsistent allocation bases across segments.** If Segment A uses a Total Cost Input G&A base and Segment B uses Value-Added, DCAA will scrutinize whether this is a legitimate difference or a CAS 401 consistency violation.

**Missing home office reconciliation.** The consolidated ICS should reconcile total costs across all segment ICS filings and the home office ICS to the contractor's audited financial statements.

## Summary

Multi-segment ICS filings require disciplined coordination across business units. Each segment owns its own filing, but all filings must reconcile to a coherent consolidated picture. The home office allocation is the central link — get that wrong, and every downstream segment rate is incorrect. DCAA auditors reviewing a multi-segment contractor will invariably request the home office ICS alongside each segment's submission and compare them for consistency.
