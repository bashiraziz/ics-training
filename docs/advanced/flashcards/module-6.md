  {
    q: "What statute governs the limitations period for government contract cost disputes?",
    a: "The Contract Disputes Act (CDA), 41 U.S.C. §§ 7101-7109. It establishes the six-year claim accrual rule, the COFD issuance timeline, and the interest provisions applicable to contractor and government claims."
  },
  {
    q: "How long does each party have to assert a CDA claim, and when does the clock start?",
    a: "Six years from the date the claim accrues. A claim accrues when all events fixing the alleged liability have occurred and the claimant knew or should have known of the claim. For ICS disputes, this is often tied to when the ICS was submitted or when final rates were established."
  },
  {
    q: "How long does the contracting officer have to issue a Final Decision on a certified contractor claim over $100,000?",
    a: "The CO must issue the COFD within 60 days, or within that period provide a written notice of a specific date by which the decision will be issued (the latter date must still be reasonable). Failure to act constitutes a deemed denial, which the contractor may immediately appeal."
  },
  {
    q: "What certification is required for a contractor claim exceeding $100,000?",
    a: "A signed certification by a senior company official stating: (1) the claim is made in good faith, (2) supporting data are accurate and complete to the best of the signer's knowledge, and (3) the amount requested accurately reflects the contract adjustment for which the contractor believes the government is liable."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the deadlines to appeal a COFD, and to which forums?",
    a: "90 days from receipt of the COFD to appeal to a Board of Contract Appeals (e.g., ASBCA or CBCA), or 12 months from receipt to file suit in the U.S. Court of Federal Claims. Both are jurisdictional deadlines — missing them permanently bars the appeal."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the risks of leaving ICS years open for many years without closing them?",
    a: "Risks include: document retention problems (no 'final payment' to start the FAR 3-year records clock); expanding audit exposure as DCAA can still audit open years; potential loss of contractor's underbilling claims if the six-year window closes; and complications for M&A, contract closeout, and corporate restructuring."
  },
  {
    q: "How does the CDA interest provision work for contractor claims?",
    a: "Interest accrues on contractor claims from the date the claim is submitted to the contracting officer until the date the government pays. The rate is the U.S. Treasury rate established semi-annually under the Renegotiation Act. For multi-year disputes, this interest can be substantial."
  },
  {
    q: "What does 'deemed denial' mean and what can the contractor do after one?",
    a: "If the CO fails to issue a COFD within the required timeframe, the claim is 'deemed denied.' The contractor may then immediately appeal to a Board of Contract Appeals or file suit in the Court of Federal Claims — without waiting for an actual COFD."
  }
