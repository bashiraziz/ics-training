<FlashcardDeck cards={[
  {
    q: "What are the three main types of DCAA audit findings?",
    a: "1) Questioned costs — costs DCAA believes are unallowable, unreasonable, or inadequately documented. 2) Internal control findings — weaknesses in the contractor's accounting system. 3) CAS noncompliance findings — actual practices differ from the Disclosure Statement or a CAS standard requirement."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the four elements of a strong management response to a draft audit report?",
    a: "1) Address each finding individually, numbered to match the report. 2) Cite the applicable FAR or CAS regulation supporting your position. 3) Provide documentary evidence (receipts, surveys, policies). 4) Quantify your position — state what amount you accept vs. contest, and why."
  },
  {
    q: "How does a contractor typically rebut a DCAA finding on executive compensation?",
    a: "Challenge the benchmark DCAA used: confirm the peer group is appropriate for company size, industry, and geography, then provide alternative compensation surveys from recognized sources (e.g., Willis Towers Watson, McLagan) that support a higher benchmark. Document the executive's unique qualifications."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the appeal options if the ACO issues a Contracting Officer Final Decision (COFD) disallowing costs?",
    a: "The contractor can appeal to a Board of Contract Appeals (e.g., ASBCA) within 90 days of receiving the COFD, or file suit in the U.S. Court of Federal Claims within 12 months. Both deadlines are jurisdictional — missing them permanently waives appeal rights."
  },
  {
    q: "When is accepting a DCAA finding the better strategic choice?",
    a: "Accept when: the regulatory basis for disallowance is clear and documentation is weak; the disallowed amount is small relative to the cost of contesting; or accepting on a minor point builds credibility with the ACO on more important contested items."
  },
  {
    q: "How should a contractor handle a finding based on inadequate documentation when the records may still exist?",
    a: "Before responding, search all archives, email systems, credit card statements, and vendor records. If original documents are found, attach them to the management response. If genuinely lost, provide a written reconstruction explaining the cost's nature and purpose, plus any available substitute evidence (e.g., canceled checks, vendor confirmation)."
  },
  {
    q: "What distinguishes a CAS noncompliance finding from a questioned cost finding?",
    a: "A questioned cost challenges whether a specific cost is allowable or reasonable. A CAS noncompliance finding challenges whether the contractor's accounting method is consistent with the Disclosure Statement — the adjustment covers all CAS-covered contracts for the affected period, not just the sampled costs."
  }
]} />
