    q: "What FAR provision authorizes the quick-close alternative to a full ICS audit?",
    a: "FAR 42.708 authorizes quick-close, allowing the contractor and ACO to agree on final indirect cost rates without a full DCAA audit, based on billing rates already negotiated and the ACO's determination that a full audit is not cost-effective."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the four main conditions required for quick-close under FAR 42.708?",
    a: "1) No unsettled prior ICS years. 2) Total unsettled direct costs for the year are below the applicable threshold. 3) No known significant cost accounting or estimating system deficiencies. 4) Both DCAA and the ACO agree that a full audit is not warranted given the risk level."
  },
  {
    q: "What does the contractor give up by accepting a quick-close?",
    a: "The contractor waives the right to later claim additional costs not captured in the agreed final rates. If underbilled amounts are discovered after quick-close, there is no recourse — the rates are final and binding."
  },
  {
    q: "How does the quick-close timeline compare to a full audit timeline?",
    a: "Quick-close typically achieves final rates in 6-18 months from ICS submission. A full DCAA audit followed by rate negotiation typically takes 3-7 years. The difference is primarily because quick-close skips field testing, document sampling, and the full audit report process."
  },
  {
    q: "What documentation must a contractor still provide in a quick-close, even without a full audit?",
    a: "A complete ICS in standard format (all applicable schedules), the reconciliation of ICS to financial statements, and records available on request. Quick-close skips field testing but does not eliminate the need for an adequate, compliant submission."
  },
  {
    q: "When might a contractor prefer the full audit path over quick-close?",
    a: "When contested costs exist that the contractor wants formally adjudicated, when actual rates significantly exceed billing rates (making additional recovery likely), when CAS issues are pending, or when the contractor is large enough that the ACO and DCAA would not agree to quick-close anyway."
  },
  {
    q: "Who must agree for a quick-close to proceed?",
    a: "Both the ACO and DCAA must agree. The ACO has contracting authority, but DCAA's concurrence is required because DCAA is giving up audit rights. If DCAA objects — for example, because it is aware of a pending fraud referral — quick-close is not available."
