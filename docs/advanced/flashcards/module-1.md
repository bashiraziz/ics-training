  {
    q: "How does CAS 403 define a 'segment'?",
    a: "A segment is one of two or more divisions, product departments, plants, or other subdivisions of an organization that reports to a common home office and has separately identified costs with its own indirect cost pools and allocation bases."
  },
  {
    q: "How does a multi-segment contractor file its ICS?",
    a: "Each segment files its own ICS covering only that segment's direct contracts and indirect cost structure. The home office files a separate ICS for centralized costs, which are then allocated down to the segments."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the three home office cost allocation methods under CAS 403?",
    a: "1) Direct allocation — cost benefits only one segment. 2) Residual allocation — remaining costs allocated using a three-factor formula (payroll, revenue, assets) or another equitable base. 3) Special allocation — specific costs allocated by dedicated criteria (e.g., centrally managed IR&D)."
  },
  {
    q: "Why must inter-segment transactions be handled carefully in a multi-segment ICS?",
    a: "If Segment A provides services to Segment B, the transfer must be priced at cost (not market) under CAS 414/FAR 31.205-26, and the receiving segment must not include the transfer in its allocation base in a way that double-counts costs already captured at the home office level."
  },
  {
    q: "Where does a segment's share of home office costs appear in its ICS?",
    a: "The home office allocation to that segment is included as an identifiable line item in the segment's G&A indirect cost pool, typically shown on Schedule H of the segment's ICS."
  },
  {
    q: "What is the most common error when home office costs are involved in a multi-segment ICS?",
    a: "Double-counting: a segment inadvertently includes home office costs in both its own G&A pool AND its allocation base, inflating its indirect rates. DCAA cross-checks all segment ICS submissions against the home office ICS to detect this."
  },
  {
    q: "What CAS standard governs consistency of cost accounting practices across segments?",
    a: "CAS 401 requires that the contractor's cost accounting practices be consistently applied. If two segments use different G&A allocation bases, the contractor must either have a disclosed justification or DCAA may raise a CAS 401 consistency violation."
  },
  {
    q: "Why should the home office ICS be filed first or simultaneously with segment ICS submissions?",
    a: "Segments cannot finalize their G&A rates until the home office cost allocation is determined. Filing the home office ICS first (or at the same time) allows each segment to include the correct home office allocation amount in its own G&A pool."
  }
