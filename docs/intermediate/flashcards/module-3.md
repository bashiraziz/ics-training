  {
    q: "What is Schedule B, and what is its relationship to Schedule A?",
    a: "Schedule B is the detailed breakdown of each indirect cost pool. For every pool in Schedule A, Schedule B lists the individual cost elements (salaries, rent, utilities, etc.), the total incurred, amounts disallowed as unallowable, and the net allowable amount. The total allowable amount per pool in Schedule B must equal the allowable claimed amount for that pool in Schedule A."
  },
  {
    q: "What allocation base is typically used for the Fringe Benefits pool, and why?",
    a: "Total labor dollars — the sum of direct and indirect labor. The rationale is that fringe costs (health insurance, payroll taxes, retirement) benefit all employees equally, so they should be spread across all labor. This is sometimes called the 'total labor dollar' or 'total compensable labor' base."
  },
  {
    q: "What allocation base is typically used for the Overhead pool?",
    a: "Direct labor dollars (or direct labor hours). Overhead exists to support direct work, so contracts with more direct labor absorb more overhead. The base must be consistent with the contractor's disclosed accounting practices — using a different base than what was previously agreed requires an explanation and ACO notification."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the two most common G&A allocation bases, and when is each appropriate?",
    a: "Total Cost Input (TCI) — all direct costs plus allocated overhead, used when the contractor's cost structure is relatively uniform. Value-Added (VA) — TCI minus subcontract and material costs, used when large subcontract or material pass-throughs would distort the G&A rate if left in the base. Both are generally accepted by DCAA."
  },
  {
    q: "A Schedule B Overhead pool lists $22,000 in entertainment as unallowable. The auditor finds an additional $5,000 in excess per diem in the claimed travel line. How should the contractor handle this?",
    a: "The additional $5,000 in excess per diem should also be removed from the claimed amount and listed as unallowable on Schedule B with a FAR reference (e.g., FAR 31.205-46 for travel). The pool total must be reduced by $5,000 and Schedule A updated accordingly. Leaving a known unallowable cost in the claimed line is a compliance failure."
  },
  {
    q: "Why does DCAA care about year-over-year consistency in Schedule B?",
    a: "Consistency ensures that rate changes reflect real cost changes rather than accounting reclassifications. If depreciation moves from Overhead to G&A without explanation, it makes prior-year and current-year rates incomparable and could indicate an attempt to shift costs to a more favorable pool. DCAA will ask for an explanation of any material change from prior-year Schedule B structure."
  },
  {
    q: "What does the 'Reference/Basis' column in Schedule B provide, and why is it useful?",
    a: "The Reference/Basis column identifies the source document or justification for each cost element — e.g., 'Payroll records,' 'Lease agreement,' or 'FAR 31.205-14' for a disallowance. While not always formally required, it allows DCAA to verify amounts without requesting additional documentation, reducing back-and-forth during the audit."
  }
