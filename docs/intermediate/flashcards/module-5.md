  {
    q: "What is the formula for computing a rate variance dollar amount?",
    a: "Dollar Variance = (Final Rate − Provisional Rate) × Actual Allocation Base. A positive result means the contractor underbilled (government owes more). A negative result means the contractor overbilled (contractor must credit the government). This computation is performed separately for each indirect cost pool."
  },
  {
    q: "A contractor's Overhead provisional rate was 68% and the final rate is 61%. The actual direct labor base on cost-type contracts was $3,000,000. What is the dollar variance?",
    a: "(61% − 68%) × $3,000,000 = −7% × $3,000,000 = −$210,000. The contractor overbilled overhead by $210,000 and must credit the government this amount."
  },
  {
    q: "In the simplified three-pool example (Fringe +$12,000, Overhead −$28,000, G&A +$4,000), what is the net billing position?",
    a: "$12,000 + $4,000 − $28,000 = −$12,000. The contractor has a net overbilling of $12,000 and must credit the government $12,000 across its cost-type contracts."
  },
  {
    q: "Why must the rate variance be computed separately for each pool rather than using a single blended rate?",
    a: "Each pool has a different allocation base and a different set of contracts that charged to it. A single blended rate would not correctly allocate the variance to the right contracts. For example, some contracts may be large consumers of direct labor (high overhead exposure) but low users of materials (low G&A exposure). Separate pool calculations ensure each contract bears the correct share of each variance."
  },
  {
    q: "How are underbilling adjustments processed after final rates are established?",
    a: "The contractor submits final invoices to each affected contract for the additional amounts owed, based on the contract-level breakdown in Schedule E. These invoices are processed through the normal contract payment mechanism. The invoices cannot be submitted until final rates have been established by rate agreement or ACO determination."
  },
  {
    q: "How are overbilling adjustments processed?",
    a: "The contractor issues a credit to the government, typically through a negative invoice or a formal demand for payment coordinated with the ACO. Large overbillings may accrue interest charges under applicable payment regulations. The contractor cannot reduce this liability by waiting — interest may run from the time rates are established."
  },
  {
    q: "Why does a large provisional-to-final rate variance create business risk for the contractor?",
    a: "If final rates are established years after the ICS is filed and a large overbilling is discovered, the contractor must repay a substantial sum that may have already been used in operations. It can also disrupt contract closeout processes. Setting accurate provisional rates at year-start — and updating them mid-year if actuals diverge significantly — reduces this risk."
  },
  {
    q: "When does DCAA typically establish final rates — at the time of the ICS submission or later?",
    a: "Later — sometimes significantly later. The sequence is: ICS filed → adequacy review → DCAA audit → DCAA report to ACO → rate negotiations → final rate agreement. For calendar-year contractors filing by June 30, final rates are commonly not established until 18 to 36 months after filing. During this period, provisional billing continues on open contracts."
  }
]} />
