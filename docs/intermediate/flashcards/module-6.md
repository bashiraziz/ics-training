    q: "What does FAR 31.201-6 require contractors to do with unallowable costs?",
    a: "FAR 31.201-6 requires that unallowable costs be (1) identified in the accounting records, (2) excluded from any billing, claim, or proposal submitted to the government, and (3) maintained in a way that allows DCAA to verify the exclusion. In the ICS context, unallowable costs must be removed from indirect cost pools before claimed rates are computed and reflected as deductions on Schedules A and B."

    q: "What is the difference between 'expressly unallowable' and 'disputed unallowable' costs?",
    a: "Expressly unallowable costs are specifically named and declared unallowable by statute, regulation, or contract — no ambiguity (e.g., entertainment under FAR 31.205-14, lobbying under FAR 31.205-22). Disputed unallowable costs are those where the contractor believes the cost is allowable but DCAA disagrees, or where the cost principle is genuinely ambiguous as applied. The distinction matters because penalties under FAR 42.709 apply only to expressly unallowable costs."
  },

    q: "What penalty can DCAA assess if expressly unallowable costs are included in an ICS?",
    a: "Under FAR 42.709, the government may assess a penalty of two times the amount of the expressly unallowable cost, plus interest. This is in addition to disallowing the cost itself. For example, if $15,000 in lobbying costs is found in a claimed pool, the penalty is $30,000 — on top of the $15,000 disallowance."

    q: "What is the cleanest accounting method for ensuring unallowable costs are excluded from ICS pools?",
    a: "Maintaining separate G/L account codes for unallowable costs at the point of entry — for example, Account 6210 (Entertainment — Unallowable) separate from Account 6200 (Travel — Allowable). This prevents co-mingling and makes the Schedule B deduction straightforward. An alternative is a sub-ledger that flags each unallowable transaction, but this requires additional reconciliation work at year-end."
