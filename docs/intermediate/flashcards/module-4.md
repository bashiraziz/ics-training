  {
    q: "What does Schedule C show, and what must it reconcile to?",
    a: "Schedule C lists each contract active during the fiscal year and shows direct labor, direct materials, ODCs, and subcontractor costs for each. The direct labor column total must equal the direct labor allocation base used in Schedule A. Schedule C must also reconcile to the contractor's accounting system (job cost records) and to Schedule E (the over/underbilling schedule)."
  },
  {
    q: "What does Schedule D show, and how is the dollar variance computed?",
    a: "Schedule D compares the provisional billing rate to the final rate from the ICS for each pool. The dollar variance = (Final Rate − Provisional Rate) × Actual Allocation Base. A positive variance means underbilling; negative means overbilling. The sum of all pool variances in Schedule D must equal the total variance shown in Schedule E."
  },
  {
    q: "What is Schedule E, and why must it be done by contract?",
    a: "Schedule E is the claimed-versus-billed reconciliation schedule. It shows, for each cost-reimbursable contract, the difference between what should have been billed at final rates and what was actually billed at provisional rates. It must be done contract-by-contract because billing adjustments are credited or invoiced at the contract level — each contract has its own funding ceiling and payment mechanism."
  },
  {
    q: "When is Schedule F required, and what does it document?",
    a: "Schedule F is required when the contractor has cost-type subcontracts. It documents how the subcontractor's incurred cost obligation is being met — either by attaching the subcontractor's ICS data or by providing a written assertion that the subcontractor has filed or will file its own ICS with its cognizant government agency. Fixed-price subcontracts generally do not require Schedule F treatment."
  },
  {
    q: "Why does DCAA examine the organizational chart in Schedule G?",
    a: "The org chart helps DCAA verify that the indirect cost pool structure makes logical sense relative to how the company is actually organized. If a function appears in the wrong pool relative to the org chart, or if the number of indirect employees doesn't match claimed indirect salaries, DCAA will ask questions. The org chart must reflect the structure during the fiscal year being audited, not the current structure if it has changed."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the legal consequences of a false or missing Schedule H certification?",
    a: "A missing Schedule H causes an automatic adequacy deficiency — DCAA will not begin substantive review without it. A false certification can expose the signer to personal liability under the False Claims Act (31 U.S.C. § 3729), which provides for treble damages and civil penalties. The certification must be signed by an authorized official with knowledge of the financial statements."
  },
  {
    q: "What is the reconciliation chain that must hold across all ICS schedules?",
    a: "Schedule B pool totals → Schedule A allowable claimed amounts. Schedule C direct labor → Schedule A allocation bases. Schedule D dollar variances → Schedule E total variances. Schedule E contract-level adjustments → contract billing records. Each link in this chain is a DCAA reconciliation test. A break at any point is a potential deficiency."
  }
