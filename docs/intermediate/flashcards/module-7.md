<FlashcardDeck cards={[
  {
    q: "What does a DCAA deficiency letter mean for the ICS filing clock?",
    a: "A deficiency letter means DCAA has determined the submission is inadequate. The six-month filing clock stops as of the original submission date and does not restart until an adequate resubmission is accepted. DCAA cannot begin substantive audit work until adequacy is confirmed. The contractor must identify and fix all deficiencies before resubmitting."
  },
  {
    q: "What is the most automatic ICS deficiency — the one that requires no analysis to catch?",
    a: "A missing or unsigned Schedule H (contractor certification). DCAA will not accept any ICS without a signed certification from an authorized company official. This is a threshold requirement — if Schedule H is absent or improperly signed, the submission is inadequate regardless of how good the rest of the package is."
  },
  {
    q: "A contractor's Schedule A shows $2,100,000 in the Overhead pool. The sum of all Schedule B Overhead line items is $2,087,000. What deficiency does this create and how is it resolved?",
    a: "This is a reconciliation deficiency — Schedule A does not equal Schedule B for the Overhead pool. The $13,000 gap has no explanation. Resolution: identify the source of the discrepancy (e.g., a late journal entry captured in A but not B, or a rounding error), correct the schedules so both show the same figure, and document the reconciliation in a supporting worksheet."
  },
  {
    q: "What deficiency arises when a contractor changes its G&A allocation base from Total Cost Input to Value-Added without explanation?",
    a: "An allocation base consistency deficiency under FAR 42.703. The ICS must use the allocation base that was established in the contractor's disclosed accounting practices or negotiated rate agreement. Switching bases without notifying the ACO and providing a justification is a deficiency. The contractor must either revert to the prior base or document and justify the change."
  },
  {
    q: "What is Deficiency 6 (Direct Costs Don't Reconcile to Contract Billing Records), and what schedule usually surfaces it?",
    a: "This deficiency occurs when the direct costs in Schedule C for a contract don't match what was actually invoiced on that contract. Schedule E (the over/underbilling reconciliation) usually surfaces the gap — the computed variance doesn't match the billing records. Prevention: prepare Schedule E before finalizing Schedule C to catch mismatches early."
  },
  {
    q: "A contractor provides a current org chart with its ICS, but the company reorganized mid-year. Why is this a deficiency?",
    a: "Schedule G must reflect the organizational structure during the fiscal year being audited, not the current structure. DCAA uses the org chart to assess pool structure logic and employee allocation patterns as they existed during the year under review. Providing a post-reorganization chart misrepresents the structure that drove the costs in the ICS."
  },
  {
    q: "Name three items on a thorough ICS pre-submission checklist.",
    a: "Any three of: (1) Schedule H signed by an authorized official; (2) Schedule A pool totals tie to Schedule B pool totals for each pool; (3) Allocation bases in Schedule A tie to direct cost totals in Schedule C; (4) Unallowable costs are identified, deducted on Schedule B, and tied to a sub-ledger; (5) All schedules A–H are present; (6) Org chart reflects the fiscal year structure; (7) Any year-over-year changes in pool structure are documented."
  }
