<Step moduleId="module5" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Computing Pool-Level Variances">
  <p>Denali Technical Group has the following data for its fiscal year. Use this information to compute the rate variance for each pool.</p>

  | Pool | Provisional Rate | Final Rate | Allocation Base (cost-type contracts) |
  |---|---|---|---|
  | Fringe Benefits | 30% | 33% | $2,500,000 |
  | Overhead | 75% | 70% | $1,800,000 |
  | G&A | 14% | 15% | $4,200,000 |

  <Question answer="Fringe: (33% − 30%) × $2,500,000 = +3% × $2,500,000 = +$75,000 (underbilled). Overhead: (70% − 75%) × $1,800,000 = −5% × $1,800,000 = −$90,000 (overbilled). G&A: (15% − 14%) × $4,200,000 = +1% × $4,200,000 = +$42,000 (underbilled). Net = $75,000 − $90,000 + $42,000 = +$27,000 net underbilling. Denali is owed an additional $27,000 by the government across all cost-type contracts.">
    Compute the dollar variance for each pool. Show which pools were underbilled and which were overbilled. What is Denali's net billing position?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Yes — the net underbilling of $27,000 must be broken down by contract through Schedule E. Each contract gets a share of the variance based on the direct costs it incurred (and thus the amount of indirect costs allocated to it). A contract that drove 25% of the direct labor base absorbs 25% of the Overhead variance. This is critical because each contract has its own funding ceiling — a contract with limited remaining funding may not be able to absorb a large underbilling without a modification.">
    Does the net underbilling of $27,000 mean Denali submits one invoice for $27,000? Or must the adjustment be handled differently?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module5" stepId="2" title="Step 2: A Large Overbilling Scenario">
  <p>Denali's Overhead pool was provisionally billed at 75%, but the final rate is only 70%. During the year, Denali's direct labor on cost-type contracts was $1,800,000, but its invoices were submitted using the provisional rate on a monthly basis. The ACO is now asking Denali to explain and resolve the overbilling.</p>
  <Question answer="The Overhead overbilling = (70% − 75%) × $1,800,000 = −$90,000. This occurred because Denali's overhead costs were lower than projected — perhaps indirect headcount was lower than budgeted, or facility costs came in under budget. The provisional rate of 75% was set based on projections that did not materialize, so every invoice submitted during the year overclaimed overhead by approximately 5 cents on the dollar of direct labor.">
    Why did the Overhead overbilling occur? Walk through the economic logic of how the provisional rate created the overpayment.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Denali must credit the government $90,000 for the Overhead overbilling, allocated across the affected contracts per Schedule E. This is processed as a negative invoice (credit) through the contract payment system, or as a demand for repayment coordinated by the ACO. If there is a net underbilling across all pools ($27,000), Denali's total credit is reduced: it credits $90,000 in overhead but submits $75,000 + $42,000 = $117,000 in additional fringe and G&A billings. Net: $117,000 − $90,000 = $27,000 additional payment to Denali.">
    How is the $90,000 Overhead overbilling resolved in practice? How does it interact with the underbillings in Fringe and G&A?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module5" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Timing and Business Impact">
  <p>Denali filed its ICS on June 28. DCAA completed its adequacy review and determined the submission was adequate. The ICS is now in audit. Denali has two open contracts that will be completing performance in the next 90 days.</p>
  <Question answer="Denali cannot submit its final billing adjustment invoices yet — even though the ICS shows a $27,000 net underbilling. Final rates are not established until DCAA completes its audit, issues a report to the ACO, and the ACO negotiates and executes a final rate agreement. Until then, Denali can only continue billing at provisional rates on open contracts. The $27,000 is a receivable that Denali knows it is owed but cannot collect until the rate settlement process is complete — which could take another 12 to 24 months.">
    Denali knows it is owed $27,000 on net underbilling. Can it invoice the government for this amount now? Why or why not?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="For contracts completing performance soon, Denali should: (1) Ensure the contracts are not administratively closed before the rate adjustment is processed — premature closure can make recovery more difficult. (2) Notify the ACO of the expected contract completion and flag the pending rate settlement so the ACO can coordinate with DCAA to prioritize the audit for those contracts. (3) Keep billing records and cost data organized and accessible because DCAA auditors will need to audit those contracts as part of the closeout process. Contractors sometimes specifically request expedited DCAA review for contracts nearing physical completion.">
    What practical steps should Denali take to protect its ability to collect the underbilling on contracts that are about to complete performance?
  </Question>
</Step>
