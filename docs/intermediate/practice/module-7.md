<Step moduleId="module7" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Spotting Deficiencies in a Submission Package">
  <p>Ironclad Analytics has submitted its ICS to DCAA. You are performing an internal pre-review using the deficiency checklist. Here is what you find:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Schedule A is present and complete.</li>
    <li>Schedule B is present. Overhead pool total on Schedule B: $1,840,000. Overhead allowable claimed on Schedule A: $1,840,000. ✓</li>
    <li>Schedule C is present. Direct labor total: $2,200,000. Overhead base in Schedule A: $2,200,000. ✓</li>
    <li>Schedule D is present and computed.</li>
    <li>Schedule E is present but the total variance is $62,000. Schedule D computed total dollar variance: $74,000.</li>
    <li>Schedule F: Ironclad has no cost-type subcontracts. No Schedule F provided.</li>
    <li>Schedule G: Org chart provided, but it is dated two years ago — before the company reorganized.</li>
    <li>Schedule H: Signed by the Director of Contracts, not the CFO or a company officer.</li>
  </ul>
  <Question answer="Three deficiencies: (1) Schedule E total ($62,000) does not equal Schedule D computed variance ($74,000) — a $12,000 reconciliation failure. This is a standard adequacy deficiency. (2) Schedule G org chart is two years old and does not reflect the current organizational structure during the fiscal year under review. DCAA needs the structure as it existed during the year. (3) Schedule H is signed by the Director of Contracts. Unless this person has documented authority to sign on behalf of the company (board resolution or written delegation), the certification may not be from an 'authorized official' — an automatic adequacy deficiency.">
    Identify all deficiencies in this submission. For each, name the deficiency category from Module 7.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Missing Schedule F is not a deficiency here — Ironclad has no cost-type subcontracts, so Schedule F is not required. A brief statement noting this fact is good practice (e.g., 'Ironclad has no cost-type subcontracts during the fiscal year; Schedule F is not applicable'). DCAA will not cite the absence of Schedule F as a deficiency when there are genuinely no cost-type subcontracts, as long as this is clear from the submission.">
    Ironclad has no cost-type subcontracts and provided no Schedule F. Is this a deficiency?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module7" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Diagnosing the Schedule D/E Mismatch">
  <p>The $12,000 gap between Schedule D ($74,000 computed variance) and Schedule E ($62,000 total contract variances) must be resolved. The Overhead pool has a final rate of 72%, provisional rate of 68%, and base of $2,200,000.</p>
  <Question answer="Schedule D: (72% − 68%) × $2,200,000 = 4% × $2,200,000 = $88,000. Wait — the scenario states Schedule D shows $74,000. Check: if the base is $1,850,000 (not $2,200,000), then 4% × $1,850,000 = $74,000. This suggests a possible error: Schedule A and C show a base of $2,200,000 but Schedule D used $1,850,000. Alternatively, the Overhead rate variance may not be 4% in Schedule D. In either case: the Schedule D base or rate must be verified against Schedule A. The Schedule E total must be rebuilt contract-by-contract from Schedule C data. The correct Schedule D variance, computed from verified rates and base, should be $88,000 (at 4% × $2,200,000) — suggesting both Schedule D ($74,000) and Schedule E ($62,000) may have errors and both need correction.">
    Verify the Schedule D computed dollar variance using the given rates and base. Does Schedule D's $74,000 appear correct? What investigation is needed?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Ironclad should: (1) Re-pull the Schedule D computation and verify the base figure used matches Schedule A/C ($2,200,000). (2) Rebuild Schedule E from scratch using final rate 72% and provisional rate 68% applied to each contract's actual direct labor from Schedule C. (3) Confirm that the sum of Schedule E contract variances equals the recomputed Schedule D variance. (4) Document the reconciliation in a supporting workpaper. Only after all three schedules (D, E, and the base in A/C) are internally consistent should the resubmission be prepared.">
    What is the step-by-step process to resolve the Schedule D/E mismatch before resubmitting?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module7" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Applying the Pre-Submission Checklist">
  <p>Ironclad has fixed all three deficiencies and is ready to resubmit. Before it does, the Controller wants to run through the pre-submission checklist one final time.</p>
  <Question answer="Key checklist items to verify: (1) Schedule H — re-signed by an authorized officer (CFO or President)? (2) Schedule G — updated to reflect the organizational structure during the fiscal year? (3) Schedule D/E reconciliation — confirmed that Schedule E total equals Schedule D computed variance after corrections? (4) Schedule A/B reconciliation — still intact after any changes made during the correction process? (5) Unallowable cost sub-ledger — tied to Schedule B deductions? (6) All eight schedules (A through H, excluding F with a 'not applicable' note) are present? (7) Direct labor in Schedule C ties to Overhead base in Schedule A?">
    List the specific checklist items Ironclad must verify before resubmitting to ensure the resubmission is adequate.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="When Ironclad resubmits, DCAA will perform another adequacy review — this time checking specifically that the three cited deficiencies have been corrected. DCAA does not re-examine the entire package from scratch; it focuses on the specific deficiencies from the letter. The six-month clock restarts (or rather, DCAA records the resubmission date as the new submission date for timing purposes). If the resubmission is adequate, DCAA moves to substantive audit. If new deficiencies are introduced by changes made during the correction process, DCAA may issue a second deficiency letter — which is why the pre-submission checklist review is essential before resubmitting.">
    When Ironclad resubmits, what does DCAA do? Does it re-review the entire package or focus on the cited deficiencies?
  </Question>
</Step>
