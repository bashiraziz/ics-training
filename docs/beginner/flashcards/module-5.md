<FlashcardDeck cards={[
  {
    q: "What makes a cost 'direct' under FAR Part 31?",
    a: "A cost is direct if it can be identified specifically with a particular final cost objective — meaning it was incurred solely because of a specific contract. Examples: direct labor hours on a contract, materials purchased for a contract, subcontractor invoices for work on a contract."
  },
  {
    q: "What makes a cost 'indirect' under FAR Part 31?",
    a: "A cost is indirect if it cannot be identified specifically with a single contract but benefits multiple contracts or the business as a whole. Indirect costs are accumulated in pools and allocated across contracts using a rational base."
  },
  {
    q: "In the ICS, where do direct costs appear? Where do indirect costs appear?",
    a: "Direct costs appear on Schedule C (listed by contract). Indirect costs appear on Schedules A and B — pooled by category on Schedule B, with pool totals and rates summarized on Schedule A."
  },
  {
    q: "What is the FAR consistency rule for cost classification?",
    a: "Under FAR 31.202, if a type of cost is treated as direct on one contract, it must be treated as direct on all contracts where the same circumstances exist. You cannot charge costs directly to some contracts and indirectly to others without a documented, consistently applied policy."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the three most common indirect cost pools for government contractors?",
    a: "Fringe Benefits (employer payroll taxes, health insurance, leave, retirement), Overhead (costs supporting direct work: supervision, facilities, indirect labor), and General & Administrative / G&A (costs of running the overall business: executive salaries, accounting, legal)."
  },
  {
    q: "What is the most common G&A allocation base, and what does 'total cost input' mean?",
    a: "Total cost input is the most common G&A base. It means all costs incurred — direct costs plus fringe and overhead — before G&A is applied. Using this base spreads G&A across everything the company spends, not just direct labor."
  },
  {
    q: "Name three unallowable cost types under FAR Part 31.205 that must be excluded from ICS schedules.",
    a: "Entertainment and amusement costs (FAR 31.205-14), lobbying and political activity costs (FAR 31.205-22), and interest on borrowings (FAR 31.205-20). These must be removed from indirect pools before any costs are claimed."
  },
  {
    q: "Why is including unallowable costs in indirect pools the most common ICS mistake?",
    a: "Because unallowable costs often live in the same accounting system accounts as allowable costs. Without deliberate identification and removal before filing, they flow into Schedule B as claimed costs — which is a billing error the government can recover, and in willful cases, a False Claims Act violation."
  }
]} />
