<FlashcardDeck cards={[
  {
    q: "Which ICS schedule reconciles billed costs to claimed costs?",
    a: "Schedule E — the reconciliation of claimed vs. billed costs by contract. It is the financial settlement document of the ICS."
  },
  {
    q: "What are the three columns on Schedule E?",
    a: "Total costs billed (invoiced using provisional rates during the year), total costs claimed (actual costs at final indirect rates), and the variance (billed minus claimed)."
  },
  {
    q: "If a contractor billed $420,000 but only incurred $395,000 in costs, what is the position and what must happen?",
    a: "The contractor overbilled by $25,000. The contractor owes the government a refund, typically processed as a credit or repayment through the contracting officer."
  },
  {
    q: "If a contractor billed $310,000 but actually incurred $327,500, what is the position and what can the contractor do?",
    a: "The contractor underbilled by $17,500. The contractor can submit a final invoice for the additional $17,500 after final rates are approved."
  },
  {
    q: "Why can't a contractor just net all overbillings and underbillings across contracts into a single check?",
    a: "Because contracts may be with different agencies, have different funding limits, and require individual contracting officer authorization. Each contract's variance must be settled through that contract's own payment mechanism."
  },
  {
    q: "What are two common causes of Schedule E variances?",
    a: "1) Actual indirect pool costs running higher or lower than the provisional estimate. 2) The allocation base (e.g., direct labor) changing relative to forecast — a smaller base spreads the same pool costs at a higher rate."
  },
  {
    q: "What is a risk of waiting too long to collect an underbilling after final rates are set?",
    a: "Delays can create complications with contract funding, closeout, and contracting officer availability. Contractors should not wait years to pursue final billing after rates are approved."
  }
]} />
