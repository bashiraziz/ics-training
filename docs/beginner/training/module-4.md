## The ICS Is a Package, Not a Form

New filers often expect the ICS to be a single government form. It isn't. The ICS is a structured package of schedules — each one capturing a specific view of the contractor's cost data. Together, they allow DCAA to verify that costs are accurate, allowable, and properly allocated.

While the exact number of schedules can vary by contractor complexity and agency requirements, every complete ICS includes the core schedules described below. DCAA's adequacy checklist requires all of them to be present before the submission is considered adequate.

## Schedule A: Summary of Indirect Costs

**What it contains:** A high-level summary of each indirect cost pool — the total claimed costs for Fringe, Overhead, G&A, and any other pools the contractor maintains. Schedule A shows the pool total, the allocation base, and the resulting indirect rate.

**Who uses it:** DCAA auditors use Schedule A as the starting point for rate verification. The contracting officer uses it to understand the big picture before approving final rates.

**Key point:** The rates on Schedule A are the final rates the ICS is establishing. They must be computable directly from the data presented.

## Schedule B: Indirect Expense Detail

**What it contains:** A line-by-line listing of every cost element within each indirect pool. For each expense account — salaries, rent, utilities, depreciation — Schedule B shows the amount claimed and identifies the pool it belongs to.

**Who uses it:** This is the primary audit document for testing individual cost elements. DCAA will trace costs on Schedule B back to the general ledger and supporting documentation (invoices, payroll records, timesheets).

**Key point:** Unallowable costs must be excluded from Schedule B. If entertainment expenses, lobbying costs, or other FAR Part 31.205 unallowable items appear in the accounting system, they must be removed before being claimed here.

## Schedule C: Direct Costs by Contract

**What it contains:** A contract-by-contract listing of all direct costs charged during the fiscal year. Columns typically include direct labor, materials, subcontractor costs, travel, and other direct costs (ODCs) for each contract.

**Who uses it:** DCAA uses Schedule C to verify that direct costs are properly segregated from indirect costs and that no costs have been double-counted (charged both directly and through an indirect pool).

**Key point:** The sum of all contract direct costs on Schedule C, combined with the indirect allocations from Schedule A, should reconcile to the contractor's total claimed costs.

## Schedule D: Indirect Cost Rates Summary

**What it contains:** A side-by-side comparison of provisional billing rates and final indirect rates for each pool. Schedule D is where the rate variance is formally documented.

**Who uses it:** This is the rate settlement document. The contracting officer and DCAA use Schedule D to see exactly where rates changed and by how much.

**Key point:** Schedule D makes the true-up visible. A large variance between provisional and final rates signals either that estimates were significantly off or that cost behavior changed during the year — both of which DCAA will want to understand.

## Schedule E: Claimed vs. Billed Costs by Contract

**What it contains:** For every contract, Schedule E shows total costs billed using provisional rates, total costs actually incurred (claimed at final rates), and the net overbilling or underbilling.

**Who uses it:** The contracting officer uses Schedule E to process billing adjustments — collecting overbillings and paying underbillings. DCAA uses it to verify that the reconciliation math is correct.

**Key point:** Schedule E is the financial settlement document. The net position across all contracts determines whether the contractor writes a check or submits a final invoice.

## Schedule F: Subcontract Information

**What it contains:** A listing of subcontracts performed under the contractor's government prime contracts, including subcontractor name, contract number, and costs incurred.

**Who uses it:** Used to verify that subcontract costs on Schedule C are properly supported and that pass-through arrangements are disclosed.

**Key point:** Not all contractors have subcontracts. If applicable, this schedule must be complete and consistent with Schedule C.

## Schedule G: Organizational Chart

**What it contains:** The contractor's organizational chart for the fiscal year covered by the ICS.

**Who uses it:** DCAA uses it to understand the business structure, identify cost centers, and evaluate whether the indirect pool structure makes sense given how the company is organized.

## Schedule H: Contractor Certification

**What it contains:** A signed statement by a senior company official certifying that the ICS is accurate and complete to the best of their knowledge.

**Who uses it:** This is the legal cornerstone of the submission. False statements on a certified ICS can trigger False Claims Act liability.

**Key point:** The certification must be signed by an individual with authority to bind the company — typically a CFO, Controller, or designated officer. An unsigned or improperly signed certification renders the submission inadequate.

## Schedule Summary Table

| Schedule | Contents | Primary User |
|---|---|---|
| A | Indirect pool totals and final rates | DCAA, Contracting Officer |
| B | Line-item indirect expense detail | DCAA auditors |
| C | Direct costs by contract | DCAA, Contracting Officer |
| D | Provisional vs. final rate comparison | Contracting Officer, DCAA |
| E | Billed vs. claimed costs by contract | Contracting Officer |
| F | Subcontract information | DCAA |
| G | Organizational chart | DCAA |
| H | Contractor certification (signed) | Legal/compliance record |
