## Meet NovaTech Solutions

NovaTech Solutions is a small 8(a) IT services contractor headquartered in northern Virginia. For fiscal year 2024 (January 1 – December 31, 2024), NovaTech had three active government contracts:

- **Contract 1:** CPFF task order with Army, $900,000 ceiling
- **Contract 2:** T&M contract with DHS, $600,000 ceiling
- **Contract 3:** CPFF contract with HHS, $400,000 ceiling

Total government contract revenue for the year: approximately **$1.8 million**. All three contracts involve cost reimbursement, and total expected incurred costs far exceed the $250,000 threshold. NovaTech must file an ICS.

## NovaTech's Indirect Cost Structure

NovaTech maintains three indirect cost pools — the standard structure for a company of its size:

- **Fringe Benefits Pool:** Employer payroll taxes, health insurance, paid leave, retirement match
- **Overhead Pool:** Indirect labor (a program manager supporting multiple contracts), facilities costs, IT systems used for contract work
- **G&A Pool:** CEO and CFO salaries, accounting fees, legal expenses, office supplies, business development

The allocation bases are:
- Fringe rate base: Total direct and indirect labor dollars
- Overhead rate base: Direct labor dollars
- G&A rate base: Total cost input (direct costs + fringe + overhead)

## Provisional Rates for FY 2024

At the start of FY 2024, NovaTech's provisional billing rates (based on FY 2023 actuals) were:

| Pool | Provisional Rate | Base |
|---|---|---|
| Fringe Benefits | 28% | Total labor dollars |
| Overhead | 42% | Direct labor dollars |
| G&A | 12% | Total cost input |

NovaTech used these rates on every invoice submitted to Army, DHS, and HHS throughout 2024.

## What Actually Happened in FY 2024

When NovaTech's CFO, Maria Cho, closed the books on December 31, 2024, the actual numbers told a slightly different story:

| Pool | Actual Pool Costs | Actual Base | Final Rate |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fringe Benefits | $198,400 | $708,571 total labor | **28.0%** |
| Overhead | $147,000 | $590,000 direct labor | **24.9%** |
| G&A | $108,250 | $901,750 total cost input | **12.0%** |

Fringe and G&A came in right at the provisional rates — good estimating. But overhead came in at **24.9%** versus the provisional rate of **42%**. Why? NovaTech had budgeted for a new program manager hire in March 2024, but the position wasn't filled until October. Eight months of that salary never hit the overhead pool. The facility lease was also renegotiated downward mid-year.

## Computing the Overbilling

Because overhead was billed at 42% all year but the final rate is 24.9%, NovaTech overbilled overhead costs on all three contracts. Here is Schedule E:

| Contract | Costs Billed | Costs Claimed | Variance |
|---|---|---|---|
| Contract 1 (Army CPFF) | $487,200 | $427,050 | +$60,150 (overbilled) |
| Contract 2 (DHS T&M) | $341,400 | $298,725 | +$42,675 (overbilled) |
| Contract 3 (HHS CPFF) | $218,700 | $191,475 | +$27,225 (overbilled) |
| **Total** | **$1,047,300** | **$917,250** | **+$130,050** |

NovaTech collected **$130,050 more** in overhead billings than it actually incurred. That money belongs to the government.

## Preparing and Filing the ICS

Maria and NovaTech's outside accountant began preparing the ICS in March 2025 — three months before the June 30, 2025 deadline. The package included:

- **Schedule A:** Summary showing final rates of 28.0% / 24.9% / 12.0%
- **Schedule B:** Line-by-line listing of all indirect expenses, with unallowable costs (one holiday party expense of $2,200) removed from the fringe pool
- **Schedule C:** Direct labor, travel, and subcontract costs for each of the three contracts
- **Schedule D:** Provisional vs. final rate comparison — the overhead variance was clearly documented with a written explanation of the delayed hire
- **Schedule E:** The reconciliation table above showing $130,050 in net overbillings
- **Schedule H:** Signed by NovaTech's CFO on June 20, 2025

The ICS was submitted electronically to the cognizant DCAA field audit office on **June 24, 2025** — six days before the deadline.

## DCAA's Desk Review

Within three weeks, DCAA's auditor, Robert, completed the adequacy review. His checklist confirmed:

- All schedules present and complete
- Schedule H properly signed by CFO
- Rates computable from Schedule A/B data
- Schedule E reconciles to Schedule C
- The holiday party ($2,200) was correctly removed from the claimed fringe pool

DCAA found the submission **adequate**. NovaTech's relatively simple cost structure and clean documentation placed the submission in the limited-scope audit category. Robert's primary area of focus was the overhead pool composition, specifically verifying that the reduced rate was genuinely caused by the delayed hire and lease renegotiation — not by indirect costs being inappropriately reclassified as direct charges.

After documentation review, DCAA accepted the overhead rates as submitted. Final rates were agreed upon, and the contracting officer for each contract processed the billing adjustments. NovaTech issued credits totaling $130,050 — applied proportionally across the three contracts through modified invoices.

## Lessons Learned

**1. Estimate carefully.** The overhead variance here was explainable and well-documented, which made audit resolution straightforward. An unexplained 17-point drop in overhead would have triggered much more scrutiny.

**2. Remove unallowables before you file.** NovaTech caught the holiday party expense during ICS preparation. Had it remained in the claimed fringe pool, DCAA would have questioned it — and questioned what else might be lurking.

**3. Start early.** Beginning preparation in March gave NovaTech time to find and fix the unallowable cost, reconcile the schedules, and have the CFO review everything before signing. Contractors who start in late June rarely produce adequate submissions.

**4. Document rate variances proactively.** Attaching a brief written explanation of the overhead variance to Schedule D gave DCAA the context it needed without a back-and-forth request cycle. Transparency speeds up resolution.

**5. The ICS is annual.** NovaTech must go through this process every year. The systems, documentation habits, and cost allocation practices they build for FY 2024 make FY 2025 easier.
