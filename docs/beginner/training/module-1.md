## The Short Version

An Incurred Cost Submission (ICS) is the annual package a government contractor files with the Defense Contract Audit Agency (DCAA) to reconcile what it actually spent during the fiscal year against what it billed the government using estimated rates. Think of it as a tax return for government contracts — except instead of calculating what you owe the IRS, you're calculating whether your billing rates were too high or too low.

The formal name is **Incurred Cost Submission** or **ICS**. You may see older references to "ICE" (Incurred Cost Electronically), which was DCAA's submission tool. The submission itself is always called the ICS.

## Why the ICS Exists: FAR 52.216-7

The legal foundation for the ICS is **FAR 52.216-7**, the "Allowable Cost and Payment" clause. This clause is incorporated into cost-reimbursable and time-and-materials contracts and requires contractors to:

1. Bill using **provisional (estimated) indirect rates** during the year
2. Submit an ICS after the fiscal year ends to establish **final indirect rates** based on actual costs
3. Settle any difference between what was billed and what was actually incurred

Without this reconciliation, the government would have no way to confirm that the indirect costs billed during the year reflect what the contractor actually spent. The ICS closes that loop.

## What "Indirect Rates" Actually Means

Contractors don't bill every cost item-by-item on cost-type contracts. Instead, they charge **direct costs** (labor, materials, subcontracts tied to a specific contract) plus **indirect costs** (overhead, fringe benefits, G&A expenses) expressed as rates applied to a base.

For example, a fringe benefit rate of 30% means: for every $1.00 of direct labor, add $0.30 in fringe costs. During the year, the contractor uses a **provisional rate** — a pre-approved estimate. At year-end, the ICS computes the **final rate** using actual numbers. The difference is settled up.

## Provisional Rates vs. Final Rates

| Feature | Provisional Rate | Final Rate |
|---|---|---|
| **When set** | Before or during the fiscal year | After fiscal year end |
| **Based on** | Estimates or prior-year actuals | Actual costs incurred |
| **Purpose** | Used for billing invoices during the year | Used to settle the true cost |
| **Who approves** | DCAA or Contracting Officer | Established through ICS audit/negotiation |
| **Status** | Temporary | Permanent for that fiscal year |

## What the ICS Package Contains

The ICS is not a single form — it is a package of schedules. The core schedules cover:

- A summary of indirect cost pools and claimed amounts
- Detailed expense listings for each pool
- Direct costs charged to each government contract
- A comparison of provisional rates billed vs. final rates computed
- A contract-by-contract reconciliation of billed vs. claimed costs
- Supporting certifications and organizational information

Later modules cover each schedule in detail.

## Who Uses the ICS and How

**DCAA** receives the submission and performs an audit or desk review. Their job is to verify that costs are allowable, allocable, and reasonable under FAR Part 31.

**Contracting Officers (COs)** rely on DCAA's findings to approve final rates and authorize settlement payments. If the contractor overbilled, the CO issues a demand for repayment. If underbilled, the contractor can invoice the additional amount.

**The contractor's accounting team** is responsible for preparing the ICS accurately, maintaining supporting documentation, and responding to DCAA inquiries.

## A Practical Example

Suppose your company billed $500,000 in overhead during the year using a provisional rate of 85%. At year-end, your ICS shows actual overhead costs of $450,000 against the same base — a final rate of 78%. You collected $500,000 but should have collected only $459,000. The ICS documents that difference, and you refund approximately $41,000 to the government.

That reconciliation — documented, auditable, and signed under penalty of law — is exactly what the ICS is designed to accomplish.
