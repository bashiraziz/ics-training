<Step moduleId="module1" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Identify the Legal Basis">
  <p>Your colleague hands you a government contract and says, "We're required to file an ICS for this contract every year." You want to verify that claim by finding the clause that creates the obligation.</p>
  <Question answer="FAR 52.216-7, the 'Allowable Cost and Payment' clause. This clause is incorporated into cost-reimbursable and time-and-materials contracts and requires contractors to bill using provisional rates during the year and submit an annual ICS to establish final indirect rates.">
    Which FAR clause creates the obligation to file an Incurred Cost Submission, and what type of contracts contain it?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="The contractor bills using provisional (estimated) indirect rates during the year, submits an ICS after fiscal year-end to report actual indirect costs and compute final rates, and then settles any difference between what was billed and what was actually incurred — refunding overbillings or invoicing underbillings.">
    In plain terms, what three-step process does FAR 52.216-7 require contractors to follow?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module1" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Apply the Tax Return Analogy">
  <p>A new employee on your contracts team asks you to explain what the ICS actually does. You want to use a plain-English analogy before getting into the regulatory details.</p>
  <Question answer="The ICS is like a tax return for government contracts. Just as a tax return reconciles estimated tax payments made during the year against what you actually owed, the ICS reconciles provisional billing rates (the estimates) against actual indirect costs incurred (the reality). The result determines whether you get money back or owe more.">
    Explain the ICS using the tax return analogy. What does the "estimated tax payment" correspond to, and what does the final tax liability correspond to?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="DCAA (Defense Contract Audit Agency) receives and audits the ICS. The Contracting Officer approves final rates and processes billing adjustments. The contractor's accounting team prepares the ICS, maintains supporting documentation, and responds to DCAA inquiries.">
    Who are the three main parties involved with the ICS, and what does each one do with it?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module1" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Analyze a Simple Rate Scenario">
  <p>Apex Services used a provisional overhead rate of 90% applied to direct labor for its entire fiscal year. It billed $800,000 in direct labor and collected overhead accordingly. When the ICS was completed, actual overhead costs were $680,000 against the same $800,000 direct labor base.</p>
  <Question answer="The final overhead rate is $680,000 ÷ $800,000 = 85%. Apex billed overhead at 90% ($720,000 collected) but only incurred 85% ($680,000). The overbilling is $720,000 − $680,000 = $40,000. Apex must refund $40,000 to the government.">
    Calculate Apex's final overhead rate, the total overhead billed, and the overbilling amount that must be refunded.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Provisional rates are pre-approved estimates set before or during the year, used for billing invoices. They are temporary placeholders. Final rates are computed from actual costs after the fiscal year ends and are permanently fixed for that year. The ICS is the process that produces final rates and reconciles the difference from provisional billing.">
    What is the key difference between provisional rates and final rates, and which document establishes the final rate?
  </Question>
</Step>
