<Step moduleId="module6" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Build Schedule E">
  <p>Pinnacle Defense Consulting has three active cost-type contracts. The ICS is complete and final indirect rates have been established. Here is the data for each contract:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Contract Alpha (Army CPFF): Billed $612,000 using provisional rates. Final costs at approved rates: $588,000.</li>
    <li>Contract Beta (Navy T&M): Billed $289,500 using provisional rates. Final costs at approved rates: $301,200.</li>
    <li>Contract Gamma (DoD CPFF): Billed $175,000 using provisional rates. Final costs at approved rates: $175,000.</li>
  </ul>
  <Question answer="Contract Alpha: $612,000 billed − $588,000 claimed = +$24,000 (overbilled — Pinnacle owes the government $24,000). Contract Beta: $289,500 billed − $301,200 claimed = −$11,700 (underbilled — Pinnacle can invoice $11,700 more). Contract Gamma: $175,000 − $175,000 = $0 (balanced). Total: $612,000 + $289,500 + $175,000 = $1,076,500 billed; $588,000 + $301,200 + $175,000 = $1,064,200 claimed; Net variance = +$12,300 (net overbilling).">
    Complete the Schedule E reconciliation table. For each contract, calculate the variance (billed minus claimed), identify the position (overbilled or underbilled), and compute the net position across all three contracts.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="The rate variance driving the overbilling on Contract Alpha is most likely that the provisional overhead or G&A rate was higher than the final rate — the company billed more indirect cost per dollar of direct work than it actually incurred. For Contract Beta, the underbilling means a pool came in higher than the provisional estimate — perhaps G&A expenses ran over budget, or the direct labor base shrank (raising the rate). These are the same dynamics from Module 3: final rates reflect actuals, not estimates.">
    What does the Contract Alpha overbilling suggest about the relationship between provisional and final indirect rates? What might explain Contract Beta's underbilling?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module6" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Process the True-Up">
  <p>Final rates are now approved. The contracting officers for each contract must be involved in settling the variances. Pinnacle's CFO suggests they just send one net check to DoD for $12,300 and be done with it.</p>
  <Question answer="The CFO's approach is incorrect. Each contract's variance must be settled through that contract's own payment mechanism and with that contract's Contracting Officer. Contract Alpha (Army) and Contract Gamma (DoD) cannot automatically offset Contract Beta (Navy). The agencies are different, the funding is different, and the COs are different people with authority over their own contracts. Netting across agencies without authorization could create unauthorized obligation or payment issues.">
    Why is the CFO's "net check to DoD" approach incorrect? What is the proper way to settle variances across three contracts with potentially different agencies?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="For Contract Alpha: The Army CO will issue a demand for repayment of $24,000, or Pinnacle will apply a $24,000 credit against an open invoice on that contract. For Contract Beta: Pinnacle submits a final indirect cost invoice to the Navy CO for $11,700, referencing the approved final rates. Contract Gamma requires no action. Each transaction is documented with a reference to the approved rate agreement or DCAA audit report.">
    Describe the specific true-up action required for each of Pinnacle's three contracts. What documents support each action?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module6" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Diagnose a Schedule E Error">
  <p>Before submitting the ICS, Pinnacle's reviewer notices that a fourth contract — Contract Delta (CPFF with State Department) — is not on the Schedule E at all. Contract Delta had $95,000 in activity during the year. The accounting team says they forgot to include it because it "barely had any costs."</p>
  <Question answer="Omitting Contract Delta is a material error. Schedule E must include every cost-reimbursable and T&M contract active during the fiscal year, regardless of dollar size. If Contract Delta had $95,000 in costs and was billed using provisional rates, there may be a billing variance that must be settled. A missing contract makes the ICS incomplete, potentially renders it inadequate, and leaves an open billing that could affect contract closeout.">
    What is the problem with omitting Contract Delta from Schedule E? What could go wrong if the ICS is submitted without it?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Pinnacle should add Contract Delta to Schedule E before submitting. They need to determine the provisional costs billed during the year for Contract Delta and compare them to the final costs at approved indirect rates. The variance (overbilling or underbilling) must be calculated and settled through the State Department CO just like the other contracts. The omission should be corrected before submission — not discovered by DCAA, which would raise questions about the thoroughness of Pinnacle's ICS preparation.">
    How should Pinnacle correct the omission before filing? What information do they need to complete the Contract Delta line on Schedule E?
  </Question>
</Step>
