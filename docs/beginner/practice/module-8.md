<Step moduleId="module8" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Analyze the Overhead Rate Variance">
  <p>NovaTech's provisional overhead rate was 42% applied to direct labor. The final overhead rate came in at 24.9% — a 17-point drop. The direct labor base for the year was $590,000.</p>
  <Question answer="Total overhead billed: 42% × $590,000 = $247,800. Total overhead earned at final rate: 24.9% × $590,000 = $146,910. Total overbilling: $247,800 − $146,910 = $100,890. (Note: The module scenario allocated this across three contracts totaling $130,050, which includes the full loaded rate true-up across fringe, overhead, and G&A. The overhead component alone calculates to approximately $100,890 at the direct labor base given.)">
    Calculate total overhead billed during the year and total overhead earned at the final rate. What is the overhead overbilling on a direct-labor base of $590,000?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="The rate dropped because the budgeted program manager hire was delayed — the position wasn't filled until October, leaving eight months of expected salary out of the overhead pool. The facility lease was also renegotiated downward mid-year. Both factors reduced actual overhead costs significantly compared to the estimate. The key lesson: rate variances should always be explainable by specific, identifiable business events. A clear explanation in Schedule D is what made NovaTech's audit resolution smooth.">
    What specifically caused NovaTech's overhead rate to drop 17 points from provisional to final? Why was explaining this variance in Schedule D important?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module8" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Apply NovaTech's Lessons to a New Scenario">
  <p>You are the Controller at SkyBridge Federal, a company similar in size to NovaTech. It is February 2026. Your fiscal year ended December 31, 2025, and you've never filed an ICS before. Your G&A pool contains the following items recorded during 2025: CEO salary ($195,000), accounting fees ($28,000), holiday office party ($4,800), professional association dues ($3,200), and business development travel ($22,000).</p>
  <Question answer="The holiday office party ($4,800) is unallowable under FAR 31.205-14 (entertainment costs) and must be removed. Business development travel ($22,000) requires review — travel costs for legitimate business development activities are generally allowable under FAR 31.205-46, but travel costs for entertainment or social purposes are not. Professional association dues ($3,200) are generally allowable under FAR 31.205-43 if related to the contractor's business. CEO salary ($195,000) and accounting fees ($28,000) are generally allowable G&A costs. The minimum required removal before Schedule B is the $4,800 party cost.">
    Review the G&A pool for SkyBridge. Identify any unallowable costs that must be removed before entering the pool on Schedule B. Cite the relevant FAR Part 31.205 subsection for each item you flag.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Following NovaTech's approach: (1) Start now (February) — four to five months before the June 30 deadline. (2) Gather all accounting records and identify all cost-type and T&M contracts active in 2025. (3) Classify costs as direct (Schedule C) or indirect (Schedules A and B). (4) Remove all unallowable costs from indirect pools. (5) Compute final indirect rates (pool ÷ base for each pool). (6) Build Schedule D comparing provisional rates to final rates and write explanations for any significant variances. (7) Complete Schedule E by reconciling billed vs. claimed costs for each contract. (8) Have the CEO or CFO sign Schedule H. (9) Submit by June 30, 2026. The most important lesson from NovaTech: start early, remove unallowables, and document every variance.">
    Using NovaTech's experience as a model, outline the nine-step ICS preparation process SkyBridge should follow to ensure an adequate, on-time filing for fiscal year 2025.
  </Question>
</Step>
