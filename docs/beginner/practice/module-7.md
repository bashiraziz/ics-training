<Step moduleId="module7" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Navigate the Adequacy Review">
  <p>Longbow Systems submitted its ICS on June 25. Ten days later, DCAA sends a written deficiency notice identifying three issues: (1) Schedule H is signed by the VP of Sales, not an officer with financial authority; (2) The G&A pool total on Schedule A does not match the sum of line items on Schedule B; (3) Schedule F is missing entirely, though Longbow had two subcontractors during the year.</p>
  <Question answer="All three deficiencies make the submission inadequate. (1) Schedule H must be signed by someone with authority to bind the company financially — the VP of Sales typically does not have that authority. An improperly signed certification is a fundamental deficiency. (2) If the pool total on Schedule A can't be reproduced from Schedule B data, the rates are not verifiable — a core adequacy requirement. (3) Schedule F is required when subcontracts exist; its absence means the submission is incomplete. DCAA will return the submission; it is not considered filed until a complete, adequate package is received.">
    Evaluate each deficiency. Does each one independently render the submission inadequate, or is only one of them disqualifying? What must Longbow do next?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="DCAA's adequacy review is a completeness check — it does not verify the accuracy or allowability of the numbers, only that the required elements are present and internally consistent. A submission can pass adequacy and still have significant audit findings. 'Adequate' does not mean 'approved' or 'correct.' It only means DCAA can begin its substantive review.">
    DCAA accepts a refiled submission as adequate three weeks later. Does "adequate" mean the rates are approved? What does adequate actually mean?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module7" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Respond to DCAA Findings">
  <p>Longbow's ICS enters a limited-scope audit. DCAA's auditor focuses on the G&A pool and identifies two items she is questioning: (1) $8,200 in "client entertainment" expenses included in the G&A pool, and (2) $15,000 in "conference attendance" costs that the auditor says lack adequate documentation.</p>
  <Question answer="For the $8,200 entertainment costs: These are unallowable under FAR 31.205-14 (entertainment costs). Longbow should not contest this finding — the costs are clearly unallowable and should have been removed before filing. Longbow should acknowledge the finding and agree to the disallowance. For the $15,000 conference costs: If the conferences were business-related and allowable under FAR 31.205-43 (trade, business, technical, and professional activities), Longbow should respond in writing citing FAR 31.205-43, provide conference agendas, registration receipts, and business justification for each conference. Documentation resolves this type of finding without disallowance.">
    How should Longbow respond to each of the two audit findings? What documentation should accompany the response to the $15,000 conference cost question?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="DCAA has no contract authority — it cannot unilaterally disallow costs or demand payment. DCAA issues findings and recommendations in its audit report. The Contracting Officer reviews the report and the contractor's response, then makes the binding determination on whether costs are allowed or disallowed. If Longbow disagrees with the CO's determination, it has contractual dispute rights under the Contract Disputes Act. DCAA's finding is a recommendation, not a final ruling.">
    If DCAA questions $23,200 in G&A costs, does that mean Longbow must immediately refund $23,200? What is DCAA's actual authority here?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module7" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Analyze Audit Risk Factors">
  <p>Longbow's ICS shows that its overhead rate jumped from 58% last year to 89% this year — a 31-point increase. The explanation in Schedule D states only: "Overhead increased due to business growth." No detailed breakdown of what drove the increase is provided.</p>
  <Question answer="A 31-point jump in overhead rate is a significant audit trigger. DCAA tracks rate trends year-over-year and a change of this magnitude with a vague explanation ('business growth') is exactly the type of anomaly that escalates a limited-scope audit to a full audit. Auditors will want to understand what specific costs entered the overhead pool that weren't there before, whether any indirect charges should actually be direct charges, and whether any unallowable costs were captured in the pool during a period of growth.">
    Why is a 31-point year-over-year overhead rate increase an audit trigger? What specific questions will DCAA likely ask about this change?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Instead of the vague explanation provided, Longbow should include a detailed written narrative in Schedule D explaining: which specific cost elements drove the increase (e.g., two new indirect support hires, new facility lease, new software systems), why those costs are allowable and properly classified as overhead, and why the direct labor base stayed relatively flat while overhead grew (if applicable). Specific, documented explanations with dollar amounts prevent the rate change from becoming an extended audit focus. Transparency is far more effective than brevity when explaining unusual variances.">
    What should Longbow have included in its Schedule D explanation to reduce audit risk? Describe what a strong rate variance explanation looks like.
  </Question>
</Step>
