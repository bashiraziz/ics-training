<Step moduleId="module3" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Compute Final Rates">
  <p>Cascade Analytics closed its fiscal year and gathered the following actual cost data for its three indirect pools:</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Fringe Benefits pool: $215,000 actual costs; allocation base (total labor) = $750,000</li>
    <li>Overhead pool: $330,000 actual costs; allocation base (direct labor) = $600,000</li>
    <li>G&A pool: $120,000 actual costs; allocation base (total cost input) = $1,000,000</li>
  </ul>
  <Question answer="Fringe rate: $215,000 ÷ $750,000 = 28.7%. Overhead rate: $330,000 ÷ $600,000 = 55.0%. G&A rate: $120,000 ÷ $1,000,000 = 12.0%. These are the final indirect rates that will appear on Schedule A and be compared to provisional rates on Schedule D.">
    Calculate Cascade's final indirect rate for each of the three pools. Show your work.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="The allocation base matters because a smaller base spreads the same pool costs across fewer dollars, producing a higher rate. For example, if Cascade's direct labor shrank from $700,000 to $600,000 but overhead costs stayed at $330,000, the rate rose from 47.1% to 55.0% — not because spending increased but because the base shrunk. Rate changes can be driven by base changes as much as pool cost changes.">
    Cascade's overhead base (direct labor) was lower than expected during the year. Explain why a shrinking allocation base can drive a higher final rate even if pool spending didn't increase.
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module3" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Identify Rate Variances">
  <p>Cascade's provisional billing rates were: Fringe 30%, Overhead 50%, G&A 11%. Compare these to the final rates computed in Step 1.</p>
  <Question answer="Fringe: provisional 30% vs. final 28.7% — overbilling (provisional was higher, contractor collected too much). Overhead: provisional 50% vs. final 55% — underbilling (provisional was lower, contractor collected too little). G&A: provisional 11% vs. final 12% — underbilling (provisional was lower). Two of the three pools resulted in underbillings, which means Cascade can bill additional amounts for those pools after final rates are established.">
    For each pool, identify whether Cascade has an overbilling or underbilling, and state the direction of the variance.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="The overhead rate went from a provisional 50% to a final 55% — a 5-percentage-point increase. This means for every $1,000 of direct labor, Cascade billed $500 in overhead but should have billed $550. This is an underbilling of $50 per $1,000 of direct labor. Cascade can submit a final invoice for the additional overhead costs it incurred but didn't bill during the year.">
    If Cascade billed $600,000 in direct labor during the year using a provisional overhead rate of 50%, how much additional overhead can it invoice after the 55% final rate is confirmed?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module3" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Trace the True-Up Process">
  <p>Final rates have been agreed upon. Cascade now needs to process the billing adjustments on its two active cost-type contracts through the true-up process.</p>
  <Question answer="The true-up process works as follows: (1) Final rates are documented in a rate agreement or DCAA audit report. (2) Schedule E is completed using approved final rates, showing billed vs. claimed costs by contract. (3) For contracts where billed exceeds claimed (overbilling), the CO issues a demand for repayment or the contractor credits a future invoice. (4) For contracts where claimed exceeds billed (underbilling), the contractor submits a final indirect cost invoice for the additional amount. (5) Once settled, the contract proceeds to financial closeout.">
    Walk through the five-step true-up process after final rates are established. What happens at each step?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="Once final rates are set for a fiscal year, they are permanently fixed. They cannot be reopened or revised later, absent fraud or a documented clerical error. This is why it's critical to get the ICS right — the rates established through the audit process are the final word on that year's indirect cost recovery.">
    After the contracting officer approves Cascade's final indirect rates, can those rates be changed later if Cascade finds an accounting error? Explain.
  </Question>
</Step>
