<Step moduleId="module2" stepId="1" title="Step 1: Determine Filing Obligation">
  <p>Meridian Systems has four active government contracts during its fiscal year (October 1, 2024 – September 30, 2025):</p>
  <ul>
    <li>Contract A: Firm-Fixed-Price IT support, $2.1M</li>
    <li>Contract B: Cost-Plus-Fixed-Fee research contract, $190,000 expected incurred costs</li>
    <li>Contract C: Time-and-Materials software development, $310,000 expected incurred costs</li>
    <li>Contract D: Firm-Fixed-Price hardware procurement, $500,000</li>
  </ul>
  <Question answer="Yes, Meridian must file an ICS. Contract B (CPFF, $190,000) and Contract C (T&M, $310,000) are cost-reimbursable and T&M contracts. Together, their expected incurred costs total $500,000, which exceeds the $250,000 threshold. Even if assessed separately, Contract C alone at $310,000 exceeds the threshold.">
    Does Meridian have a filing obligation? Identify which contracts count toward the threshold and explain your reasoning.
  </Question>
  <Question answer="The ICS is due six months after September 30, 2025 — the end of Meridian's fiscal year. That means the deadline is March 31, 2026. This is not a calendar-year contractor, so the June 30 deadline does not apply.">
    What is Meridian's ICS deadline? Show how you calculated it.
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module2" stepId="2" title="Step 2: Evaluate Contract Types">
  <p>A colleague argues that Meridian's FFP contracts (A and D) should also be included in the ICS because they involve large dollar amounts. You need to explain why contract type — not dollar amount — determines ICS applicability.</p>
  <Question answer="FFP contracts do not trigger ICS filing because there is no reimbursement of actual indirect costs. The price is fixed at award regardless of what the contractor actually spends. There are no provisional billing rates to reconcile because the government pays the fixed price, not actual costs plus indirect allocations.">
    Why are FFP contracts excluded from the ICS filing obligation even when they involve significant dollar amounts?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="For IDIQ contracts, the filing obligation depends on the type of individual task orders issued under the IDIQ vehicle. A task order written as CPFF or T&M triggers the ICS requirement for that task order's costs. A task order written as FFP does not. You must look at each task order's contract type, not just the IDIQ vehicle itself.">
    How would you evaluate an IDIQ (Indefinite-Delivery / Indefinite-Quantity) contract's ICS filing obligation?
  </Question>
</Step>

<Step moduleId="module2" stepId="3" title="Step 3: Consequences of Late or Inadequate Filing">
  <p>It is now March 28, 2026 — three days before Meridian's deadline. The Controller tells you the submission is "basically done" but the Schedule H certification hasn't been signed yet, and one of the indirect pools on Schedule B doesn't reconcile. She wants to submit what they have now and send corrections later.</p>
  <Question answer="Submitting an inadequate ICS is treated as not filing for deadline purposes. DCAA will return it with a deficiency notice, and the clock restarts from the resubmission date — not the original submission date. This means Meridian will effectively miss the March 31 deadline even though it attempted to file on time. The unsigned Schedule H alone makes the submission inadequate.">
    What will happen if Meridian submits the incomplete package on March 28 and plans to fix it later?
  </Question>
  <Question answer="DCAA can recommend that the contracting officer withhold 5% of amounts invoiced on cost-type contracts until the ICS is filed. On Meridian's $500,000 in active cost-type contract billings, a 5% withhold is $25,000 — real cash withheld from every future invoice until the adequate ICS is submitted. This creates immediate cash flow pressure in addition to the audit relationship damage.">
    If Meridian misses the deadline, what is DCAA's available remedy and what is the practical financial impact on Meridian?
  </Question>
</Step>
